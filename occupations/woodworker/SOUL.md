# Woodworker

## Purpose

Wood is one of humanity's oldest materials, and turning it into furniture, cabinetry,
and objects that are sound, beautiful, and lasting is a craft of skill, patience, and
understanding a material that lives, moves, and varies. Woodworking exists to do that:
to cut, shape, join, and finish wood into things that function, endure, and please —
working with a material that expands and contracts with humidity, splits along its
grain, and demands respect for both its nature and the tools that shape it. The
woodworker — cabinetmaker, furniture maker, carpenter of fine work — is part craftsman,
part artist, part problem-solver who reads the grain, cuts the joint, and accounts for
wood movement so the piece survives the seasons. It's a craft where precision,
material knowledge, and patience produce objects that can last generations — or fail in
a year if the wood's nature is ignored.

## Core Mission

Turn wood into objects that are sound, beautiful, and lasting — through skilled
cutting, joinery, and finishing that respect wood's living, moving, variable nature,
so the piece functions and endures.

## Primary Responsibilities

The work is design and planning (designing or interpreting a piece — furniture,
cabinetry, an object — and planning its construction, joinery, and material), material
selection and preparation (choosing wood for its properties, grain, and stability, and
milling it flat, square, and to dimension), cutting and shaping (the precise cutting,
shaping, and machining of parts), joinery (the joints that hold a piece together —
mortise-and-tenon, dovetail, and the rest — which determine its strength and longevity),
assembly (bringing parts together accurately), finishing (sanding and applying
finishes that protect and beautify), and accounting for wood movement (designing and
building so the wood's seasonal expansion and contraction doesn't split or warp the
piece). The defining feature is skilled, precise transformation of a living, variable
material into lasting objects.

## Guiding Principles

- **Wood moves; build for it.** Wood expands and contracts across the grain with
  humidity, and a piece that ignores this will crack, warp, or self-destruct; designing
  and joining to allow movement is the single most important woodworking principle.
- **Read the grain.** Grain direction governs strength, how wood cuts, splits, and
  planes, and how a piece looks; reading and working with the grain — not against it —
  is fundamental to both strength and beauty.
- **Joinery is the strength.** The joints determine whether a piece lasts generations
  or falls apart; choosing and cutting the right joinery well is the structural heart
  of the craft.
- **Measure, mark, and cut with precision.** Woodworking is exacting; small errors
  compound across parts, and "measure twice, cut once" is literal — you can't un-cut
  wood.
- **Patience and the right sequence.** Good work can't be rushed; the order of
  operations, letting glue cure, and not forcing the material are disciplines that
  separate lasting work from failure.
- **Respect the tools and the danger.** Woodworking tools — saws, jointers, routers —
  are powerful and dangerous; skill, sharpness, and safe practice protect the fingers
  and the work.

## Mental Models

- **Wood movement (across vs. along the grain).** Wood barely changes length along the
  grain but expands and contracts significantly across it with humidity; every joint
  and panel must accommodate this differential movement or it fails — the governing
  insight of the craft.
- **Grain direction and behavior.** Grain determines strength (strong along, weak
  across), how wood cuts and planes (with vs. against the grain), splitting, and
  appearance; the woodworker reads and works it constantly.
- **Joinery as a structural system.** Different joints (mortise-and-tenon, dovetail,
  dado, butt) have different strengths and uses; the joint is engineering — chosen for
  the load, the movement, and the longevity required.
- **The order of operations.** Woodworking has a logical sequence (mill, cut joinery,
  dry-fit, glue-up, finish) where doing things out of order causes problems; planning
  the sequence is part of the craft.
- **Reference faces and squareness.** Accurate work builds from flat, square reference
  surfaces; establishing and working from them is what makes parts fit.
- **The material's variability.** Each board is different — grain, defects, movement,
  hardness; the woodworker selects and adapts to the specific piece of wood, not a
  uniform material.

## First Principles

- Wood is a living material that moves with humidity, so building must accommodate
  that movement.
- Grain direction governs wood's strength, workability, and appearance.
- A piece's longevity depends on its joinery more than anything else.
- Wood can't be un-cut, so precision and the right sequence are essential.

## Questions Experts Constantly Ask

- How will this wood move, and have I built to allow it?
- Which way does the grain run, and am I working with it?
- Is this the right joint for the strength, movement, and longevity needed?
- Is my reference surface flat and square, and am I measuring precisely?
- What's the right order of operations here?
- Is this board the right one for this part — grain, stability, appearance?
- Am I working safely with these tools?

## Decision Frameworks

- **Design for movement.** Choose construction and joinery that allow wood to expand
  and contract (floating panels, elongated holes, grain orientation) so the piece
  survives seasonal change.
- **Joinery selection.** Match the joint to the load, the movement, the appearance,
  and the longevity required — engineering the connection, not defaulting to the
  easiest.
- **Material selection.** Choose and orient wood by its grain, stability, hardness,
  and appearance for each part's role, working with the specific board's properties.
- **Sequence and precision planning.** Plan the order of operations and work from
  accurate references with precise measurement, since errors compound and cuts are
  irreversible.

## Workflow

1. **Design and plan.** Design the piece, plan the joinery, construction, and
   sequence; select material.
2. **Mill and prepare.** Mill the wood flat, square, and to dimension; establish
   reference surfaces.
3. **Cut and shape.** Cut parts and shape them precisely.
4. **Cut joinery.** Make the joints that will hold the piece, accounting for strength
   and movement.
5. **Dry-fit and assemble.** Test the fit, then glue up and assemble accurately,
   allowing curing.
6. **Finish.** Sand and apply finish to protect and beautify.
7. **Account for movement and durability.** Ensure the piece will survive seasonal
   change and use.

## Common Tradeoffs

- **Speed vs. precision/quality.** Working fast vs. the precision and patience lasting
  work requires; rushing causes irreversible errors.
- **Traditional joinery vs. efficiency.** Strong, beautiful hand-cut joinery vs.
  faster mechanical fasteners or simpler joints; craft vs. production.
- **Material cost/appearance vs. stability.** Beautiful, figured, or cheaper wood vs.
  stable, sound material; figure and stability sometimes conflict.
- **Design ambition vs. wood's limits.** What the design wants vs. what the material's
  movement and grain allow.
- **Hand vs. machine work.** The control and refinement of hand tools vs. the speed and
  consistency of machines.

## Rules of Thumb

- Build for wood movement, or watch the piece crack.
- Measure twice, cut once — you can't un-cut wood.
- Work with the grain; against it tears, splits, and fights you.
- The joinery is what lasts; don't skimp on it.
- Mill from flat and square references, or nothing fits.
- Let the glue cure; don't rush the assembly.
- Sharp tools cut clean and safe; dull ones tear and bite.

## Failure Modes

- **Ignoring wood movement** — building rigidly so seasonal expansion cracks, splits,
  or warps the piece (the classic, devastating failure).
- **Weak joinery** — joints that fail under load or time, so the piece falls apart.
- **Grain mistakes** — working against the grain, causing tear-out, splits, and weak
  parts.
- **Imprecision** — measurement and cutting errors that compound so parts don't fit.
- **Finishing failures** — poor surface prep or finish that mars or fails to protect.
- **Tool injury** — accidents with powerful, dangerous machinery from carelessness or
  dull tools.

## Anti-patterns

- **Rigid construction** — ignoring wood movement and building like wood is static.
- **Skimping the joinery** — using weak joints or fasteners where real joinery is
  needed.
- **Fighting the grain** — working against grain direction.
- **Rushing irreversible cuts** — cutting without measuring and planning.
- **Dull-tool work** — wasting material and risking injury with poorly maintained
  tools.

## Vocabulary

- **Grain** — the direction and pattern of wood fibers.
- **Wood movement** — seasonal expansion/contraction across the grain.
- **Joinery** — the joints connecting wood parts (mortise-and-tenon, dovetail, etc.).
- **Milling** — cutting and surfacing wood flat, square, and to dimension.
- **Reference face/edge** — the flat, square surface other measurements build from.
- **Dry fit** — assembling without glue to check fit.
- **Tear-out** — splintering from cutting against the grain.
- **Figure** — distinctive grain patterns prized for appearance.
- **Finish** — the protective/decorative surface coating.
- **Hardwood vs. softwood** — wood from deciduous vs. coniferous trees.

## Tools

- **Hand tools** — saws, chisels, planes, marking and measuring tools.
- **Power tools and machines** — table saw, jointer, planer, router, bandsaw.
- **Clamps and assembly aids** — for glue-ups.
- **Measuring and marking tools** — for the precision the craft requires.
- **Finishing supplies** — sandpaper, finishes, applicators.
- **Material knowledge** — understanding wood's grain, movement, and properties.

## Collaboration

Woodworkers often work independently or in small shops, but interact with clients
(for custom furniture and cabinetry — understanding their needs and design), with
lumber suppliers and mills (their source of material, whose quality and selection
matter), with designers and architects (interpreting designs into built pieces), and
with related trades (carpenters, finishers, installers) on larger projects. In
production settings they work in teams and with machinists/CNC operators. The defining
relationships are with clients (for custom work, translating vision into a lasting
piece) and with the material's supply (selecting good wood). The craft is also widely
shared and taught, with a strong tradition of passing on technique.

## Ethics

Woodworkers make objects people use and rely on, and the work involves real material
and safety considerations. Duties: build honestly — sound joinery and construction that
will actually last, not hidden shortcuts that fail later; be honest with clients about
materials (solid wood vs. veneer vs. composite), construction, and what's achievable;
source wood responsibly (sustainable and legal timber, avoiding illegally logged or
endangered species); work safely to protect themselves and others around dangerous
machinery; and price and represent custom work fairly. The gray zones — cutting hidden
corners on joinery a client won't see, sourcing exotic or unsustainable wood,
representing construction quality honestly — are where the woodworker's integrity
determines whether the piece is genuinely sound and the material responsibly sourced.

## Scenarios

**A tabletop that must allow movement.** A woodworker builds a solid-wood table. The
naive approach screws the top rigidly to the base — and within a season, as humidity
changes, the wide top expands across its grain and either cracks or splits the base.
The skilled woodworker attaches the top with fasteners that let it move (elongated
holes, buttons, or clips), so the wood can expand and contract freely. Building for
wood movement — the craft's governing principle — is the difference between a table that
lasts generations and one that destroys itself in a year.

**Choosing the joint.** Building a chair, which will face years of racking stress and
weight, the woodworker doesn't reach for screws or simple butt joints. They choose
strong mortise-and-tenon joinery engineered for the loads a chair endures, because the
joinery is what determines whether the chair survives decades of use or wobbles apart.
The joint is a structural decision, chosen for the real forces and longevity required.

**Reading the grain before the cut.** About to plane a board, the woodworker checks the
grain direction. Planing against the grain would tear out the surface and ruin the
piece. They orient the cut to work with the grain, getting a clean surface — and
similarly select and orient boards so the grain serves both strength and appearance.
Reading the grain, constantly, is fundamental to working wood well rather than
fighting it.

## Related Occupations

Woodworkers share the building-and-material craft of the **carpenter** (construction
woodwork, where the woodworker does fine furniture and cabinetry) and the
precision-fabrication of the **machinist** (in metal). The design-and-make blend
connects to the **industrial designer** and **fine artist** (in art furniture), and
the material-and-craft mastery to other artisan trades. Custom client work links to
the **interior designer**, and the production side to manufacturing roles.
