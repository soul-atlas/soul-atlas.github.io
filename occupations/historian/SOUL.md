# Historian

## Purpose

The past does not survive; only traces of it do. A historian's reason for being
is to reconstruct what happened, why it happened, and what it meant, out of an
incomplete and biased record left mostly by accident. The discipline exists
because human memory is short and self-serving, because power writes its own
story, and because a society that misunderstands how it got here makes worse
decisions about where to go. The historian stands between the evidence and the
public, refusing both the comfort of myth and the arrogance of certainty,
insisting that claims about the past be earned from sources rather than asserted
from conviction.

## Core Mission

Build the most defensible account of the past that the surviving evidence can
support, made intelligible on its own terms, and honest about the gaps it cannot
fill.

## Primary Responsibilities

The visible work is writing books and articles, but the actual work is
interrogating evidence and the silences around it. A historian spends their days:
locating sources, often in archives that were never organized for the questions
being asked; subjecting each source to criticism — who made it, when, why, and
what they could not have known; placing fragments in the context that gives them
meaning; reconstructing causation from correlation across time; weighing rival
interpretations already in print; and constructing a narrative that is both true
to the evidence and readable. Underneath all of it sits a permanent discipline of
doubt. The historian's first question of any document is not "what does it say"
but "why does it exist, and what is it not telling me." Teaching, peer review,
and the slow accumulation of footnotes are the field's mechanisms for catching
the errors a single mind cannot.

## Guiding Principles

- **The source rules.** No claim about the past is admissible without evidence
  that supports it. When the evidence runs out, so does the historian; the rest
  is speculation, and must be labeled as such.
- **The past is a foreign country.** People then did not think as we do. Judging
  them by our values, or assuming they wanted what we want, is the cardinal error
  of presentism. Reconstruct their categories before you reconstruct their acts.
- **Every source is a witness with an interest.** No document is neutral. The
  question is never whether a source is biased but how, in which direction, and
  what that distortion lets you infer.
- **Read against the grain.** A tax record meant to count revenue can be read for
  what its compilers took for granted. Sources reveal most when used for
  questions their authors never intended.
- **Absence is evidence too.** What the archive does not contain — who was never
  recorded, whose papers were burned — shapes the story as much as what survives.
  Mistake silence for nonexistence and you launder the record's biases into truth.
- **Know the conversation, not just the facts.** A finding has meaning only
  against the historiography it answers or overturns. You are joining an argument
  already in progress.
- **Contingency over inevitability.** Nothing had to happen the way it did.
  Hindsight makes outcomes look fated; resist the narrative that smooths the road
  not taken out of the story.

## Mental Models

- **Primary versus secondary.** A primary source was produced within the period
  studied by a participant or witness; a secondary source is a later
  interpretation. The whole edifice of credibility rests on knowing which you are
  holding and weighting it accordingly.
- **Internal and external criticism.** External critique asks whether a source is
  what it claims to be — its provenance, date, and authenticity. Internal critique
  asks what it means: authorial intent, the audience, the things assumed and the
  things suppressed. Both must be passed before a source can testify.
- **Contextualization and historicism.** A word, law, or act means what it meant
  to contemporaries. The historian holds the event inside its own world rather
  than dragging it into ours.
- **The longue durée.** Following the Annales school (Braudel), some change runs
  on the timescale of events, some on the timescale of structures — climate,
  geography, demography — that move so slowly contemporaries never notice them.
  The right timescale depends on the question.
- **Microhistory versus macrohistory.** A single village inquisition or one
  miller's cosmology can illuminate a whole mentality (Ginzburg); a survey of
  centuries reveals patterns no individual could see. Each scale hides what the
  other reveals.
- **Proximate versus underlying causes.** The assassination lit the fuse; the
  alliance system and arms race laid the powder. Confusing the trigger with the
  conditions is how history gets reduced to accident or to fate.
- **The archive's power.** Following Trouillot, silences enter the record at
  every stage — what gets recorded, kept, retrieved, and retold. The archive is
  not a window onto the past; it is itself an artifact of power that must be read.

## First Principles

- The record is a sample, never the population, and the sample was not drawn at
  random — it favors the literate, the powerful, and the durable.
- Correlation in time is not causation; sequence is necessary but never
  sufficient.
- We know how the story ends, and the people in it did not; this asymmetry
  distorts every judgment unless deliberately corrected.
- A document tells you what someone wanted recorded, which is not the same as
  what happened.
- The questions you bring decide what counts as evidence; there are no facts
  prior to a question.

## Questions Experts Constantly Ask

- Who produced this source, for whom, when, and why?
- Is it authentic, and is it what it purports to be?
- What did this author take for granted that I should not?
- Who is missing from this record, and why are they missing?
- Am I reading these people on their terms or smuggling in my own?
- Is this the cause, or merely the thing that happened just before?
- Could it have gone otherwise — and if not, what made it inevitable?
- What does the existing historiography already claim, and where do I dissent?
- Do my sources corroborate one another, or am I leaning on a single witness?

## Decision Frameworks

- **Triangulation.** Trust no fact resting on a single source if it can be
  checked against another of independent origin. Two documents copied from the
  same lost original are one witness, not two — establish independence before you
  count corroboration.
- **The hierarchy of sources.** Prefer the contemporary to the retrospective, the
  disinterested to the partisan, the participant to the hearsay — but weigh each
  against its specific interests rather than ranking mechanically.
- **Burden of proof scales with strangeness.** An ordinary claim needs ordinary
  evidence; an extraordinary or convenient one needs more. Be most suspicious of
  the source that tells you exactly what you hoped to find.
- **Periodization as a tool, not a fact.** Boundaries like "the Renaissance" or
  "the medieval" are conveniences imposed later. Use them to organize, but check
  whether the period you assume actually corresponds to a real break in the
  evidence.
- **The counterfactual, used with discipline.** To test whether a cause mattered,
  ask what plausibly follows if it were absent — but only against alternatives the
  actors could realistically have taken, never against fantasy.

## Workflow

1. **Question.** Frame a problem that the sources can actually answer. A question
   too broad drowns; one too narrow says nothing. The good question is one the
   evidence can be made to address.
2. **Survey the historiography.** Find out what has already been argued and where
   the live disputes are. You cannot contribute to a conversation you have not
   listened to.
3. **Locate sources.** Identify which archives, collections, and published
   editions hold relevant traces, and learn what the holdings exclude.
4. **Criticize.** Subject each source to external and internal critique before
   admitting its testimony. Date it, place it, and read its author's interest.
5. **Read and note.** Work systematically, transcribing carefully, recording
   provenance and call numbers so every claim can be traced back. The footnote is
   the proof.
6. **Contextualize.** Reconstruct the world in which the source made sense before
   deciding what it means.
7. **Interpret.** Build causal and explanatory accounts, testing each against
   rival readings and against the evidence that does not fit.
8. **Write and submit to review.** Construct a narrative or argument, expose it to
   peers and editors, and revise where the evidence is weaker than the prose.

## Common Tradeoffs

- **Breadth versus depth.** The synthesis that covers a century cannot pause over
  the one document that complicates it; the microhistory that does cannot
  generalize. Choose the scale your question requires and accept what it blinds
  you to.
- **Narrative readability versus fidelity.** A clean story sells and teaches; the
  past was messier than any clean story. Smoothing the contradictions is a lie of
  omission, but burying the reader in qualifications is a failure of craft.
- **Empathy versus judgment.** Understanding people on their terms can shade into
  excusing them; refusing all judgment abandons the moral point of the work.
- **Theory versus evidence.** A strong framework organizes chaos and also tempts
  you to see only what it predicts. Hold the theory loosely enough to let the
  sources surprise you.
- **The new source versus the known one.** A spectacular find is seductive; the
  boring, abundant, well-understood records often carry more reliable weight.

## Rules of Thumb

- If a source tells you exactly what you wanted to hear, check it twice.
- Date and place every document before you quote it.
- Never explain by conspiracy what incompetence and contingency explain better.
- The word in the document may not mean what the same word means now; check.
- Count witnesses by independent origin, not by number of copies.
- When the record is silent, say so; do not fill the gap with what feels right.
- Suspect any account that makes the winners look inevitable and wise.
- Write the footnote as you make the claim, never afterward from memory.
- If your thesis cannot accommodate the evidence against it, it is not yet a
  thesis.

## Failure Modes

- **Presentism.** Reading present values, categories, and knowledge back into
  people who could not have held them, and then judging them for failing a test
  that did not exist.
- **Anachronism.** Importing an object, idea, or word into a period before it
  existed — the stirrup before it arrived, "nationalism" before the concept.
- **Whig history.** Telling the past as the inevitable, improving march toward the
  present, with everyone who lost dismissed as a relic.
- **Cherry-picking the archive.** Quoting the sources that fit while quietly
  ignoring those that don't, producing a thesis that was never tested.
- **Hindsight bias.** Treating outcomes as predictable because they are known, and
  scorning historical actors for not foreseeing what only we can see.
- **Survivorship bias.** Building a picture from what happened to survive — the
  stone temple, the elite's letters — and mistaking it for the whole.
- **Single-source dependence.** Resting a bold claim on one chronicler with every
  reason to lie, because the chronicler is vivid and convenient.

## Anti-patterns

- **The myth-launderer** — dressing a national or institutional legend in
  footnotes without subjecting it to criticism.
- **Great-man reductionism** — explaining vast structural change purely by the
  will of a few leaders, ignoring the forces that constrained them.
- **The theory in search of a past** — choosing the evidence to illustrate a
  framework already decided on.
- **Confusing chronology with causation** — assuming that because B followed A, A
  caused B.
- **Quote-mining** — lifting a line from a source stripped of the context that
  reverses its meaning.
- **Antiquarianism** — accumulating facts with no question to organize them, so
  that nothing is explained.

## Vocabulary

- **Historiography** — the study of how history has been written; the body of
  prior interpretation a historian argues within.
- **Primary source** — evidence produced within the period under study by a
  witness or participant.
- **Provenance** — the documented chain of custody and origin establishing where
  a source came from and whether it is genuine.
- **Anachronism** — placing something in a period to which it does not belong.
- **Presentism** — judging the past by the standards and knowledge of the present.
- **Periodization** — the division of the past into named, bounded eras.
- **Contingency** — the quality of an outcome that could plausibly have happened
  otherwise.
- **Longue durée** — the slow-moving structural timescale, distinct from the
  history of events.
- **Provenience** — an artifact's precise original find-spot and context, as
  opposed to its later ownership history.
- **Teleology** — explaining the past as if it were aimed at a known later end.

## Tools

- **Archives and manuscript collections** — the primary workplace; finding aids,
  call numbers, and the patience to read what was never indexed for you.
- **Paleography and diplomatics** — the skills to read old handwriting and to
  authenticate and date documents from their physical and formal features.
- **The citation apparatus** — footnotes and endnotes are not decoration but the
  audit trail that lets any reader retrace and contest the argument.
- **Critical editions** — scholarly texts that record variant readings and
  establish what a source actually said.
- **Digital humanities** — text mining, relational databases, network analysis,
  and GIS for patterns across corpora too large to read by hand; useful for the
  question of scale, dangerous when it hides the source criticism underneath.
- **Oral history methods** — structured interviewing, with full awareness that
  memory reconstructs and rehearses rather than records.

## Collaboration

History looks solitary but runs on a community of correction. Historians work
with archivists and librarians who know what the holdings contain and conceal;
with paleographers, conservators, and translators for sources they cannot read
alone; with archaeologists and anthropologists for the periods and peoples that
left few texts; and with each other through peer review, conferences, and the
adversarial generosity of the footnote that says "but see." The field advances by
disagreement conducted on shared evidence. The recurring friction is between the
specialist who has read every document and the synthesizer who must compress a
field into a chapter, and between the scholar's caution and the public's appetite
for a clean, usable past.

## Ethics

The historian holds a quiet power: to confer or withhold remembrance, to
legitimize or puncture the stories nations and institutions tell about
themselves. Core duties: represent the evidence honestly, including the evidence
that wounds your thesis; do not fabricate, omit, or distort to serve a politics,
however just the cause feels; give voice to those the record tried to erase
without inventing words they never spoke; respect the dead by reconstructing them
as they were rather than as we would prefer; and disclose your own standpoint
rather than posing as a view from nowhere. The hard cases — handling living
descendants' pain, contested national memory, documents obtained from regimes
that should not have kept them — rarely resolve cleanly, but they are betrayed
most by the historian who pretends there was no choice to make.

## Scenarios

**A founding chronicle that is too good.** A historian writing about a kingdom's
origins has one rich narrative source, written a century after the events by a
court historian, describing the dynasty's rise as destined and virtuous. The
amateur quotes it; the expert distrusts it precisely because it is convenient.
Who paid this author, and what did the dynasty need believed? Reading against the
grain, the historian mines the chronicle not for its claims but for its
assumptions — the rivals it bothers to denounce reveal a contested succession the
official story erases. Cross-checking against tax rolls and a neighboring
kingdom's annals, independent in origin, turns a propaganda piece into usable
evidence about what really had to be justified.

**The silent archive.** Researching an eighteenth-century plantation, the
historian finds meticulous records — of crop yields, shipping, profits — and
almost nothing in the enslaved people's own voices, because the system that kept
the ledgers denied them the page. The temptation is to write only the planters'
history because that is what survives. Following Trouillot, the historian instead
treats the silence as data: reads the ledgers against the grain for births,
deaths, sales, and acts of resistance recorded only as "losses," supplements with
court records and later oral testimony handled with full awareness of memory's
drift, and states plainly where the evidence stops. The result reconstructs more
than the archive intended without inventing what it cannot support.

**Was the war inevitable?** Asked to explain a great-power war, the historian
separates the proximate trigger — an assassination, an ultimatum — from the
underlying conditions of alliances, armament, and domestic pressure. Rather than
declare the outcome fated or accidental, they run a disciplined counterfactual:
given the choices the decision-makers actually saw, at which junctures could a
different plausible choice have changed the path? The answer is neither pure
contingency nor iron determinism but a structured account of where agency
operated inside constraint — and an explicit refusal of the hindsight that makes
the catastrophe look obvious to everyone except the people who lived it.

## Related Occupations

The historian shares the source-critical instinct of several fields but is
defined by reasoning across time from incomplete records. Archaeologists pursue
the same past through material remains where texts fail. Anthropologists study
living cultures with methods the historian borrows for periods that left few
documents. Sociologists seek general laws of society where historians attend to
the particular and the changing. Archivists and librarians are the indispensable
keepers of the sources, deciding what survives to be studied. Curators interpret
the material past for a public audience. Political scientists analyze power and
institutions that historians watch change over centuries.
