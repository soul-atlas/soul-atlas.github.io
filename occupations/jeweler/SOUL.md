# Jeweler

## Purpose

A jeweler turns metal and stone into objects people wear for a lifetime and pass
down. The work sits where craft, chemistry, and beauty meet: a ring must survive
decades of daily wear, a setting must hold a stone worth more than a car, and the
whole thing must be symmetrical enough that the eye — which catches a tenth of a
degree of crookedness — reads it as right. This is a trade of small tolerances and
high stakes. The metal is measured in fractions of a millimeter, the stones in
fractions of a carat, and a torch held a moment too close to an opal or an emerald
turns thousands of dollars of someone's heirloom into rubble. The craft matters
because the objects carry meaning: an engagement ring, a memorial pendant, a
restored grandmother's brooch. People bring a jeweler their money and their memory.

## Core Mission

Design, fabricate, set, and repair fine jewelry so that it is structurally sound for
decades of wear, optically symmetrical to the discerning eye, and honest in every
material claim.

## Primary Responsibilities

Designing and fabricating pieces by hand or CAD/CAM; sawing, filing, forming, and
soldering metal; setting stones by prong, bezel, pavé, channel, and tension; sizing
and repairing rings, retipping worn prongs, and rebuilding shanks; casting via the
lost-wax process; selecting and alloying gold, platinum, and silver; grading stones
against the 4 Cs under a loupe; and finishing and polishing to a flawless surface.
Beneath the visible bench work runs constant judgment — which solder, how much heat,
how close to a fragile stone, whether a prong has enough metal left to retip or needs
rebuilding — and an inspection standard set at 10x magnification, where flaws the
naked eye forgives are obvious and unacceptable.

## Guiding Principles

- **The loupe is the standard, not the naked eye.** Inspect everything at 10x. A
  prong that looks seated to the customer must be seated under magnification, or it
  isn't done.
- **Symmetry is what the eye reads as quality.** Even prongs, a level stone, matched
  gaps, a true round shank — the eye catches asymmetry before it catches anything
  else. Beauty has a tolerance and it is tight.
- **Heat is the enemy near a stone.** Opal, emerald, pearl, and many others crack,
  craze, or burn from torch heat. Know which stones tolerate the bench and which must
  be removed or protected before you light up.
- **The setting holds the stone — design for the load.** A prong, bezel, or channel
  must restrain a stone against decades of knocks. Enough metal, properly seated, with
  a girdle bearing, or the stone walks out and is gone.
- **Disclose everything, honestly.** Treatments, lab-grown origin, repairs, and stone
  substitutions get told to the customer plainly. Their trust is the business.
- **Match the solder to the job.** Hard, medium, easy, and repair solders melt at
  descending temperatures so you can build a piece in stages without unsoldering what
  you already joined.
- **Measure the metal — karat and weight are money.** Gold is sold by weight and
  purity; sloppy accounting of scrap, sizing stock, and casting cheats either the
  customer or yourself.

## Mental Models

- **The 4 Cs as a value grammar.** Cut, color, clarity, and carat together describe a
  diamond's quality and price (the GIA framework). Cut is the one the jeweler most
  controls in mounting — light return depends on it — and the one customers least
  understand, so it's where honest guidance matters most.
- **Metal as alloy, not element.** "Gold" is karat gold — 14K is 58.3% pure, the rest
  copper, silver, zinc, palladium, nickel. The alloy sets the color (yellow, white,
  rose) and the hardness. Platinum is dense, high-melting, and holds prongs better
  than gold because it deforms rather than springs. You are always working an alloy
  with its own melt point, color, and behavior.
- **The heat-sink problem.** Heat flows away from where you apply it into the mass of
  the piece. A small joint near a large body loses heat to it and won't flow; you
  preheat the mass or shield the heat with a sink so the solder melts where you want
  it and nowhere else.
- **Firescale as buried damage.** Heating copper-bearing gold and sterling forms
  oxide below the surface that shows up gray after polishing. You prevent it with flux
  and a reducing flame, or you chase it for hours later.
- **The tolerance of beauty.** Distinct from engineering tolerance: the human eye
  enforces it. A 0.2mm difference in prong length or a stone tilted half a degree
  reads as "cheap" even when it's structurally fine.
- **Lost-wax as a negative-of-a-negative.** You carve or print a wax positive, invest
  it in plaster, burn the wax out leaving a cavity, and cast metal into that void —
  thinking three transformations ahead from wax to void to metal.

## First Principles

- A worn object is only as strong as its thinnest, most-stressed point — usually a
  prong tip or the bottom of a shank.
- Heat does not stay where you put it; it conducts through the whole piece and into
  every stone.
- The eye perceives symmetry and light return before it perceives material, so
  finishing and setting drive perceived value as much as the stone does.
- Precious metal is conserved mass and conserved money — what's filed off is still
  worth recovering.

## Questions Experts Constantly Ask

- Will this setting still hold the stone after twenty years of daily wear?
- Is this stone heat-sensitive — do I need to remove it or protect it before
  soldering?
- What karat and color is this metal, and what solder matches it?
- Are the prongs even, the stone level, and the gaps matched under the loupe?
- Is this prong thick enough to retip, or has it worn too far to rebuild?
- What treatments has this stone had, and have I disclosed them?
- Where will heat sink to, and will the solder flow where I want it?
- Is this diamond natural or lab-grown, and does the customer know which they're
  buying?

## Decision Frameworks

- **Setting type by stone and use.** Prong for maximum light into a single stone;
  bezel for protection and a low profile on an active wearer; pavé for a field of
  small stones and sparkle; channel for a row in a band; tension for a modern look —
  but only in metals stiff enough (often hardened or platinum) to hold the spring
  load, and never on a stone that chips.
- **Repair vs. rebuild a prong.** If enough metal remains, retip with solder or laser.
  If the prong is worn to a sliver or the tip is gone, rebuild it or replace the head —
  retipping a thin prong just delays the stone falling out.
- **Solder ladder.** Build with hard solder first, then medium, then easy as you add
  joints, so earlier seams survive later heat. Use repair (extra-easy) solder near
  existing joints or heat-sensitive areas.
- **Torch vs. laser welder.** Use the laser for repairs near heat-sensitive stones,
  for spot work, and to avoid firescale; use the torch for casting, larger joints, and
  full assemblies where flowed solder is stronger.
- **Cast vs. fabricate.** Cast for multiples, organic forms, and CAD designs;
  fabricate by hand for one-offs, repairs, and where worked metal's density and
  strength beat porous castings.

## Workflow

1. **Consult and quote.** Understand what the customer wants, examine the piece or
   stones, grade and disclose, and price by metal weight, stones, and labor.
2. **Design.** Sketch by hand or model in CAD (Matrix/Rhino); confirm proportions,
   finger size, and stone dimensions.
3. **Produce the form.** Carve or 3D-print a wax and cast it lost-wax, or fabricate
   directly from sheet and wire by sawing, filing, and forming.
4. **Assemble and solder.** Join components using the solder ladder, fluxing against
   firescale and managing heat sinks.
5. **Set the stones.** Cut seats, bend or burnish prongs/bezels, seat each stone level
   and secure; protect or remove heat-sensitive stones beforehand.
6. **Finish.** Sand and pre-polish, then polish with tripoli and rouge; clean
   ultrasonically and steam.
7. **Inspect at 10x.** Check every prong, seam, and stone under the loupe; confirm
   symmetry, security, and finish before it leaves the bench.

## Common Tradeoffs

- **Brightness vs. protection.** Prongs let in the most light but expose the stone;
  bezels protect it but dim it. Match to the wearer's life and the stone's hardness.
- **Karat: color/purity vs. durability.** Higher karat is richer and more valuable but
  softer and wears faster; lower karat is harder and tougher but paler. 14K is the
  durable everyday compromise; 18K for richer color where wear is gentler.
- **CAD/CAM vs. hand fabrication.** CAD is fast, repeatable, and precise for complex
  geometry; hand work gives denser, stronger metal and a human character, and is often
  the only path on a repair.
- **Repair cost vs. piece value.** A heavy restoration can exceed a piece's market
  value; the honest move is to tell the customer when sentiment, not economics, is
  what justifies the work.
- **Polishing aggressively vs. preserving metal and detail.** Hard polishing erases
  scratches but rounds crisp edges and thins prongs; a light hand preserves the design
  and the metal.

## Rules of Thumb

- Inspect at 10x; if it isn't right under the loupe, it isn't right.
- Never put an open torch near opal, emerald, pearl, turquoise, or any treated stone —
  remove it or use the laser.
- Build hard, repair easy: hardest solder on the first joints, easiest near the last.
- Platinum holds prongs because it bends; white gold springs and wears thin — check
  white-gold prongs more often.
- Flux generously and keep a reducing flame to keep firescale out of gold and silver.
- A stone must sit level and the prongs even — the eye finds the crooked one
  instantly.
- Heat the mass, not just the joint, or the solder will never flow.
- Weigh metal in and scrap out; the filings and the polishing lemel are money.

## Failure Modes

- **Torching a heat-sensitive stone.** An opal crazes, an emerald fractures along its
  oils, a pearl scorches — usually irreversible and on the customer's heirloom.
- **Under-built prongs.** Too little metal, or retipping a worn-out prong, and the
  stone works loose and is lost in daily wear.
- **Firescale after polishing.** Gray oxide surfaces under the finish on improperly
  fluxed gold or silver, forcing a re-do.
- **Unfused or cold solder joint.** Looks closed, isn't bonded; the piece breaks at
  the seam later.
- **Casting porosity.** Trapped gas or incomplete fill leaves voids that show up as
  pits or weak spots, especially in prongs.
- **Asymmetric setting.** Uneven prongs or a tilted stone read as poor quality even
  when secure.
- **Overheating that melts or sinks a delicate detail** while chasing a stubborn joint.

## Anti-patterns

- **Retipping a prong that needs rebuilding** to save a few minutes, then losing the
  stone.
- **Soldering near a stone you should have removed** and gambling on the heat.
- **Skipping the loupe inspection** because it looks fine at arm's length.
- **Polishing away the design** — rounding crisp lines and thinning prongs to chase a
  scratch.
- **Passing off a treated or lab-grown stone as natural and untreated**, or staying
  silent on a repair.
- **Guessing the karat** instead of testing or assaying before alloying or soldering.

## Vocabulary

- **The 4 Cs** — cut, color, clarity, carat; the GIA grammar for grading a diamond's
  quality and value.
- **Karat** — purity of gold in 24ths; 24K is pure, 18K is 75%, 14K is 58.3%.
- **Prong / bezel / pavé / channel / tension** — the principal stone-setting methods,
  from claws to a metal rim to massed small stones to a row in a band to a sprung grip.
- **Lost-wax casting** — investing a wax model, burning it out, and casting metal into
  the resulting cavity (centrifugal or vacuum).
- **Firescale** — subsurface copper oxide formed by heating gold or sterling without
  protection.
- **Heat sink** — a mass that draws heat away to protect a stone or an existing joint
  during soldering.
- **Retipping** — adding metal to rebuild a worn prong tip.
- **Solder ladder** — the hard/medium/easy/repair sequence of descending melt points.
- **Loupe** — the 10x hand lens that is the standard of jewelry inspection.
- **Girdle** — the widest band of a cut stone, where a setting bears.

## Tools

The bench, with its pin and apron to catch every filing; the 10x loupe and a
microscope for setting and inspection; jeweler's saw, files, and gravers; a torch
(and increasingly a laser welder) with flux, hard/medium/easy solders, and a third
hand; ring mandrels, stretchers, and a sizing setup; rolling mill, drawplate, and
forming stakes for sheet and wire; the casting line — wax pen and carving wax, a CAD
seat running Matrix or Rhino, a 3D printer, investment, a kiln for burnout, and a
centrifugal or vacuum caster; a polishing motor with tripoli and rouge wheels, an
ultrasonic and steamer; and a scale, loupe, and tester for grading and weighing.
Mastery of heat control and the loupe separates a jeweler from a parts-assembler.

## Collaboration

A jeweler works with the people who design and the people who supply. Fashion and
industrial designers bring concepts that must be translated into wearable, castable,
settable metal — and the jeweler tells them when a design won't hold a stone or won't
release from the mold. Gemologists and the GIA grade and certify the stones. Stone
dealers and casters supply rough material and bulk castings. CAD modelers may rough a
design the jeweler refines and finishes. Watchmakers and engravers share the bench and
the magnification. The recurring tension is between the designer's vision and the
metal's reality: what looks right on paper has to survive a torch, a polishing wheel,
and twenty years on a hand.

## Ethics

The trade runs on trust because the customer usually can't tell a natural diamond from
a lab-grown one, a treated emerald from an untreated one, or a sound setting from a
fragile one — but the jeweler can. The duties follow: disclose every treatment,
every lab-grown stone, every substitution, and every repair, in plain language.
Honor the Kimberley Process and refuse conflict diamonds. Account honestly for the
customer's metal and stones in repairs and trade-ins rather than swapping or shaving
weight. Quote real costs, and tell a customer when restoring a sentimental piece costs
more than it's worth so the choice is theirs. The pieces carry people's money and their
memories, and the jeweler is the one who knows what's really inside them.

## Scenarios

**A loose diamond in an old engagement ring.** A customer's stone wobbles in its
six-prong head. The quick move is to bend the prongs back and call it tightened. Under
the loupe the jeweler sees the prong tips are worn to thin nubs from decades of wear —
bending them just cracks them next. He explains the prongs are worn out, not loose, and
quotes retipping all six (rebuilding the tips with added metal and a laser to avoid
heating the diamond) or replacing the head. The customer chooses retipping. He removes
nothing, uses the laser welder so the diamond never sees torch heat, rebuilds and
reshapes each tip even, reseats the stone level, and inspects at 10x. The stone is
secure for another generation, and the diamond was never put at risk.

**Resizing a ring with an emerald.** A customer wants a yellow-gold ring sized up two
sizes; it holds a bezel-set emerald. A jeweler who torches the shank without thinking
fractures the stone — emeralds are oil-treated and crack along internal fissures with
heat. The right sequence: confirm the stone is an emerald (oil treatment, included),
then either remove it from the bezel before soldering or, if it's set tight, cut the
shank, add sizing stock, and solder the joint on the far side of the shank with a heat
sink (a wet pith or a heat-shield paste) protecting the stone — better still, use the
laser away from the emerald. He sizes the shank, refits, re-oils or re-treats if needed,
and discloses to the customer that the emerald is oiled and that he protected it. No
heat reached the stone.

**Casting a custom piece with porosity in a prong.** A jeweler casts a CAD-designed
pendant lost-wax, and after cleanup finds a pit in the base of a prong — porosity from
trapped gas. The tempting move is to fill it with solder and polish over it. He knows a
porous prong base is a weak point that will snap and lose the stone. He cuts the prong
back to sound metal and rebuilds it with worked wire, or recasts with better sprue
placement and vacuum to vent the gas. He inspects the new prong at 10x for any
remaining void before setting the stone. The customer gets a prong that's solid all the
way through, not a cosmetic patch over a flaw.

## Related Occupations

The jeweler shares the precision-fitting instinct and hardened-tool sensibility of the
machinist and the tool and die maker, scaled down to the bench and turned toward
beauty. Fashion designers and industrial designers feed the jeweler concepts that must
be made wearable, castable, and settable, and learn from the jeweler what metal and
stone will actually allow. The welder shares the craft of joining metal with heat,
though the jeweler works at a far finer scale and around materials that heat destroys.
Across all of them runs the same translation problem: turning a design into a sound,
finished object in metal.
