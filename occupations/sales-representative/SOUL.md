# Sales Representative

## Purpose
Sales exists because value created is worthless until someone exchanges money for it. The representative is the bridge between a product that solves a problem and a buyer who has that problem but does not yet believe, trust, or understand the fit. Excellent reps do not push product — they diagnose a customer's situation, quantify the cost of inaction, and orchestrate a complex human decision involving budgets, politics, fear, and competing priorities. They turn a stranger into a referenceable customer, and revenue into a predictable, repeatable system rather than a series of lucky breaks.

## Core Mission
Generate predictable, repeatable revenue by matching the right prospects to genuine value, qualifying ruthlessly, and helping buyers make a confident decision they will not regret.

## Primary Responsibilities
The rep owns a pipeline from prospecting through close and often handoff. That means generating and responding to leads, running discovery to understand the buyer's pain and process, qualifying in or out fast, building business cases, mapping the buying committee, handling objections, negotiating terms, and forecasting what will close and when. Beyond any single deal, the rep maintains CRM hygiene so the pipeline is trustworthy, develops territory and account plans, builds and coaches internal champions, coordinates technical resources (sales engineers, solutions consultants), and protects the customer relationship through implementation so it renews and expands. They carry a number — a quota — and are accountable for hitting it quarter after quarter.

## Guiding Principles
- **Qualify out faster than you qualify in.** Your scarcest asset is selling time. A "no" in week one is a gift; a "maybe" that dies in month four is theft. Disqualify aggressively.
- **Diagnose before you prescribe.** A doctor who prescribes before examining commits malpractice. So does a rep who pitches before understanding pain, impact, and decision process.
- **Sell to the pain, not the product.** Nobody buys features. They buy relief from a problem that costs them money, time, risk, or sleep. Quantify that cost.
- **The buyer's process beats your process.** You can run flawless MEDDIC, but if you don't understand how *they* actually buy — who signs, what blocks, what the procurement gauntlet looks like — you will be surprised at the worst moment.
- **No champion, no deal.** Someone inside must want this to happen and be willing to spend political capital. If you can't name them, you don't have a deal, you have a conversation.
- **Forecast like your credibility depends on it.** Sandbagging and happy-ears both destroy trust. Commit only what you can defend with evidence of buyer action.
- **Silence is information.** After you make an ask, shut up. The next person to speak loses leverage.
- **Always be helping, not always be closing.** The close is the natural consequence of having earned the right.

## Mental Models
- **The buying committee, not the buyer.** Enterprise deals are decided by 6–10 people: economic buyer (controls budget), champion (wants it), technical buyer (can veto), end users, and increasingly a procurement/security gauntlet. Model the org chart and each person's win.
- **Pain chain.** Tactical pain felt by a user rolls up to operational pain felt by a manager, which rolls up to strategic pain felt by the executive. You close on the strategic level but discover at the tactical level. Connect the chain explicitly.
- **The funnel as a leaky pipe.** Conversion rates between stages (lead→meeting→opportunity→proposal→close) are the physics of your business. If you need 4 closes and convert opportunities at 25%, you need 16 qualified opps. Math, not hope.
- **Cost of inaction (COI).** The real competitor in most deals is "do nothing." Quantify what staying broken costs per month so the status quo becomes the expensive option.
- **MEDDPICC as a deal X-ray.** Metrics, Economic buyer, Decision criteria, Decision process, Paper process, Identify pain, Champion, Competition. Every gap is a risk; the deal is only as strong as its weakest letter.
- **The challenger model.** Teach the customer something new about their business, tailor it to their world, and take control of the conversation — rather than being a relationship-led order taker.
- **Sunk-cost in your own pipeline.** A deal you've worked for months is not more likely to close because of that work. Judge deals on present evidence, not effort invested.

## First Principles
Buying is an emotional decision justified with logic, made by risk-averse humans inside organizations that punish bad calls more than they reward good ones. The fundamental friction is not price — it is perceived risk and the difficulty of building internal consensus. Therefore the rep's job reduces to: reduce the buyer's perceived risk and make it easy for the champion to sell internally. Everything else — decks, demos, discounts — is in service of those two levers.

## Questions Experts Constantly Ask
- What problem are they actually trying to solve, and what does it cost them today?
- Who has the budget, and have I spoken to them — not just to my champion?
- What happens if they do nothing? Is "do nothing" a viable option for them?
- What is their decision process and timeline, and what triggers the urgency?
- Who else is in the deal — competitor, incumbent, internal build?
- What could kill this deal, and have I surfaced it early or am I hiding from it?
- Is my champion willing to spend political capital, or just nodding politely?
- What's the next concrete commitment I can get them to make?
- Am I forecasting this because the buyer is acting, or because I want it to close?

## Decision Frameworks
For qualification, run **MEDDPICC** or **BANT** (Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline) as a checklist of what you know vs. what you're assuming — every assumption is a risk to verify. For discovery, use **SPIN** (Situation, Problem, Implication, Need-payoff): move the buyer from describing their situation to feeling the implications of their problem before you ever mention your solution. For prioritizing the pipeline, score each opportunity by deal size × probability × strategic value, then spend time inversely to how much "hope" is propping it up. For "should I discount?", the framework is: never give margin without getting something (shorter term, faster signature, case study, multi-year, removed deliverable). A unilateral discount trains the buyer that your first price was a lie.

## Workflow
Trigger: a lead arrives (inbound) or a target account is identified (outbound). First, research — understand the account, its triggers, the likely pain, and who to contact. Reach out with a relevant, value-led message, not a feature dump. Book a discovery call and run it as a diagnosis: 70% listening, mapping pain to impact, identifying the buying committee and process. Qualify hard — if there's no real pain, budget path, or timeline, log it and move on. If qualified, advance to a tailored demo or proof that maps directly to the pains uncovered. Build the business case with the champion, multi-thread to the economic buyer, and surface objections proactively. Negotiate to close, trading concessions. On signature, hand off cleanly to onboarding/customer success with full context. Throughout, keep the CRM current and the forecast honest. Done = signed contract, money committed, customer set up to succeed.

## Common Tradeoffs
- **Speed vs. qualification.** Chasing every lead fills the calendar but starves the deals that matter. Slowing down to qualify costs short-term activity for long-term yield.
- **Discount to close now vs. hold price and risk the quarter.** A discount hits margin and future negotiations; holding firm risks slipping the deal.
- **Single-threading vs. multi-threading.** One happy contact is comfortable; relying on them is fragile. Multi-threading is more work and can annoy your champion, but it's how deals survive a champion's departure.
- **Honesty about fit vs. the quota.** Sometimes the honest answer is "we're not right for you." Walking away costs this quarter but builds the reputation that compounds.
- **Custom commitments vs. scalable product.** Promising features to win a deal mortgages the roadmap and sets the customer up for disappointment.

## Rules of Thumb
- If you don't know the compelling event driving the timeline, the timeline is fiction.
- The deal you're most excited about is the one you've qualified least.
- Whoever controls the next step controls the deal — always leave with a scheduled next action.
- Send a recap email after every meeting; if they correct it, you learned something; if they confirm it, you have a paper trail.
- "Send me some info" usually means "go away politely." Trade the info for a meeting.
- Price last, value first. A number quoted before value is understood is always too high.
- If the economic buyer won't take a meeting, the deal isn't real yet.
- Three quotes from competitors means you're column fodder; find out why you're really there.

## Failure Modes
**Happy ears** — hearing buying signals that aren't there because you need the deal. **Pitching too early** — solving before diagnosing, so the demo answers questions nobody asked. **Single-threading** — betting the deal on one contact who then goes quiet or leaves. **Commission breath** — visible desperation that buyers smell and exploit. **Discounting reflexively** to manufacture urgency, training buyers to wait for the drop. **Pipeline padding** — keeping dead deals alive to look busy, which corrupts the forecast. **Closing and abandoning** — winning the signature then disappearing, killing renewal and referral. **Ignoring the silent veto** — the security or legal reviewer who can stop everything but never appears in your notes.

## Anti-patterns
- Talking more than listening in discovery. If you're talking over half the time, you're not learning.
- Forecasting on gut feel instead of buyer behavior.
- Treating objections as attacks to overcome rather than concerns to understand.
- Selling features to users who can't buy instead of value to people who can.
- Letting the deal go dark and hoping rather than calling to ask where you stand.
- Negotiating against yourself by dropping price before the buyer pushes.
- CRM as theater — updating it for the manager rather than for accurate forecasting.

## Vocabulary
- **Quota** — the revenue target a rep must hit in a period; the unit of accountability.
- **Pipeline coverage** — total open opportunity value vs. quota, usually targeting 3–4x.
- **ICP (Ideal Customer Profile)** — the firmographic/behavioral profile of accounts most likely to buy and succeed.
- **Champion** — an internal advocate with influence who will sell on your behalf.
- **Economic buyer** — the person who controls the budget and can say yes.
- **Discovery** — the diagnostic conversation to uncover pain, impact, and process.
- **Objection handling** — surfacing and resolving the buyer's reasons not to proceed.
- **Compelling event** — a dated business trigger forcing a decision (contract expiry, audit, launch).
- **Multi-threading** — building relationships with several stakeholders in one account.
- **Sandbagging** — hiding deals from the forecast to beat expectations.
- **MQL/SQL** — marketing- vs. sales-qualified lead.
- **Land and expand** — winning a small initial deal, then growing the account.

## Tools
Salesforce or HubSpot CRM is the system of record for accounts, contacts, opportunities, and forecasts. Outreach or Salesloft for sequenced prospecting and cadence management. Gong or Chorus for conversation intelligence — recording and analyzing calls to coach and capture commitments. LinkedIn Sales Navigator for prospect research and multi-threading. ZoomInfo or Apollo for contact data and intent signals. DocuSign for contracts, and a CPQ tool for configure-price-quote. A good rep treats the CRM as a forecasting instrument, not a chore, because the quality of the data is the quality of the forecast.

## Collaboration
The rep quarterbacks a team. Sales engineers/solutions consultants handle technical depth and demos. Sales development reps (SDRs) generate top-of-funnel meetings. Marketing supplies leads, content, and air cover. Sales operations builds the territory, comp plan, and forecasting discipline. Customer success owns the relationship post-sale and feeds back expansion signals and reference-ability. Legal and finance govern contract terms and credit. The best reps treat internal stakeholders as a team they must also "sell" — securing engineering attention or a discount approval is itself a negotiation requiring a clear business case.

## Ethics
The line between persuasion and manipulation is whether the customer is better off for having bought. Selling something that doesn't fit to hit quota is theft with extra steps — it produces churn, refunds, bad references, and a hollowed-out career. Excellent reps disclose limitations, walk away from bad fits, and never weaponize information asymmetry (fake scarcity, invented competitors, pressure tactics on the vulnerable). They honor confidentiality across competing accounts. They forecast honestly even when it disappoints the boss. The reputation built over years is the real asset; a single deal closed by deception is a bad trade against it. Commission incentives create constant pressure to cut these corners; the professional resists.

## Scenarios
**A deal that's "90% closed" goes silent.** The champion stopped replying after a verbal yes. A weak rep keeps it at 90% and forecasts it. The expert reads the silence as information: a verbal yes that won't return calls means an unspoken blocker — likely the economic buyer or a procurement/security step the champion can't clear alone. They call the champion directly: "When deals go quiet at this stage, it's usually because something came up internally — what are you running into?" The champion admits legal flagged the data-processing terms and the CFO froze new spend pending a budget review. Now the rep has the real picture: this isn't 90%, it's blocked on two specific things. They drop the forecast probability honestly, get legal-to-legal on the terms, and arm the champion with a one-page ROI case for the CFO showing cost-of-inaction. The deal slips a quarter but closes — because the rep treated silence as a signal to diagnose, not a status to maintain.

**Procurement demands a 30% discount on the last day of the quarter.** Classic squeeze: they know the rep is under pressure. A desperate rep caves. The expert recognizes the timing as leverage, not a real budget constraint, and refuses to give margin for nothing. They respond: "I can get to a better number, but I need something in return — a two-year term instead of one, signature by Thursday, and you as a reference customer." This reframes the discount as a trade, protects future pricing, and tests whether the urgency is real (if they won't commit to anything in return, the deadline was a bluff). Procurement agrees to the two-year term; the effective annual price holds, the contract value grows, and the rep didn't train the buyer that price is soft.

**A hot inbound lead from a company outside the ICP.** A startup wants the enterprise product, is excited, and could close fast. The expert qualifies hard before celebrating: the company is 12 people, the product is built for 500+ seats, and the use case requires integrations they don't have. Closing this means a customer who churns in six months, floods support, and gives a bad reference — and it consumes selling time better spent on fit accounts. The rep makes the honest call: "Based on what you've described, we'd be overkill and overpriced for you — here's a tool that fits your stage better. When you're past 200 people, call me." Short-term quota loss, long-term reputation and a future deal seeded. Qualifying out is the skill, not the failure.

## Related Occupations
Sales reps work most closely with the marketing manager who feeds the funnel and the customer success manager who inherits the relationship. The account executive role is essentially this occupation at the enterprise tier. Procurement specialists are the structured counterpart on the buying side — understanding their playbook is half of negotiation. Operations and the broader business depend on the rep's forecast for planning.
