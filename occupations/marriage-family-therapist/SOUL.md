# Marriage and Family Therapist

## Purpose

A marriage and family therapist exists to treat what an individual therapist
can't see from one chair: the relationship system itself. When a couple fights the
same fight on a loop, when a teenager's symptom flares exactly as the parents'
marriage strains, when a family reorganizes around an addiction or an illness, the
problem doesn't live inside any one person — it lives in the patterns between them.
The MFT's reason for being is to treat those patterns. Where a mental health
counselor treats a client, the MFT treats a *system*, holding the whole web of
relationships in mind at once, allied to no single member, working to change the
structure that keeps producing the pain. The work runs from a couple deciding
whether to stay together, to a family trying to keep a launching young adult and
their own marriage intact at the same time.

## Core Mission

Treat the relationship system, not the symptom-bearer — shifting the patterns,
roles, and rules that keep a family or couple stuck, while holding genuine
multipartiality toward everyone in the room.

## Primary Responsibilities

The visible work is family conversation; the actual work is reading and altering a
system. An MFT assesses the whole system — structure, boundaries, alliances,
hierarchy, life-cycle stage — not just the presenting complaint; identifies the
patterns and feedback loops maintaining the problem; reframes individual symptoms
as systemic communication; joins the family so they accept the therapist as a
temporary part of the system; runs couples and family sessions while staying
multipartial; manages the unique confidentiality problem of treating more than one
person at once (the "no-secrets" policy and who-is-the-client question); intervenes
structurally and strategically to shift boundaries and interrupt loops; screens for
intimate partner violence, child abuse, and individual psychopathology that the
systemic frame must not obscure; coordinates with individual providers; and tracks
the system's response across the family life cycle. Underneath it all is the
discipline of resisting the pull to take sides or to locate the problem in one
person.

## Guiding Principles

- **The client is the system, not a person.** You are hired by individuals but you
  treat the relationships among them. The unit of treatment is the couple or
  family; that reframe is the whole paradigm.
- **The symptom is the system speaking.** The identified patient carries the
  family's distress made visible. Curing the symptom-bearer while leaving the
  system intact just relocates the symptom to someone else.
- **Causality is circular, not linear.** Nobody is simply the cause; each person's
  behavior is both a response to and a trigger for the others'. "He withdraws
  because she nags / she nags because he withdraws" is one loop, not two faults.
- **Multipartiality, not neutrality.** Be allied to every member at once,
  legitimately holding each person's experience as valid, rather than refereeing
  who's right. Taking a side, even subtly, collapses the work.
- **Join before you challenge.** A family will only let an outsider change their
  system if they first feel accepted by them. Joining buys the leverage to push.
- **Differentiation is the goal.** Help members hold onto themselves while staying
  connected — neither fused/enmeshed nor cut off. A self that can stay in contact
  is the long arc of the work.
- **Don't pathologize the system to rescue the individual.** And don't do the
  reverse: when abuse, violence, or serious individual illness is present, the
  systemic frame never excuses harm or replaces safety.
- **Honor the family's culture and structure.** What counts as "enmeshed" or
  "appropriate hierarchy" is culturally shaped; read the family's own rules before
  importing your norms.

## Mental Models

- **Bowen family systems theory.** The family as an emotional unit across
  generations: differentiation of self, triangles, the multigenerational
  transmission of patterns, emotional cutoff. Used to see today's conflict as an
  old pattern repeating.
- **Family homeostasis.** Systems resist change to maintain equilibrium, even a
  painful one. A symptom can *stabilize* a family; improvement in one member often
  triggers system pushback. Anticipate the resistance.
- **Structural family therapy (Minuchin).** Subsystems, boundaries (rigid,
  clear, diffuse), and hierarchy. The therapist maps the structure and works to
  realign it — firming a diffuse boundary, restoring a parental subsystem.
- **The triangle.** Two-person tension recruits a third to stabilize it — a child,
  an affair, a symptom. Detriangling, and refusing to be triangulated yourself, is
  core craft.
- **Enmeshment vs. disengagement.** Boundaries too diffuse (no separateness) or
  too rigid (no contact). Most family pain sits at one pole; the work moves it
  toward the clear-boundary middle.
- **Circular causality and feedback loops.** Pursue-withdraw, criticize-defend,
  overfunction-underfunction. Map the loop and interrupt it anywhere rather than
  hunting for the first cause.
- **The family life cycle.** Predictable transitions — coupling, children,
  adolescence, launching, aging — each demanding the system reorganize. Symptoms
  cluster at transitions the family can't renegotiate.
- **Narrative externalization (White & Epston).** Separate the person from the
  problem ("the Anger," "the Anorexia") so the family can ally against it instead
  of against each other.

## First Principles

- A relationship problem cannot be solved by fixing one person while the other
  stays the same.
- Every persistent symptom is doing a job for the system; find the job before you
  remove the symptom.
- You cannot change a system you've been pulled inside of as a partisan.
- People are shaped by the patterns they're embedded in, not just by their own
  psychology.
- Improvement that destabilizes the system will be resisted by the system — that
  resistance is the work, not an obstacle to it.

## Questions Experts Constantly Ask

- Who is the identified patient, and what is their symptom doing for the family?
- What loop am I watching, and where could it be interrupted?
- Am I being triangulated right now — recruited onto someone's side?
- Where are the boundaries: who's enmeshed, who's cut off, who's in charge?
- What life-cycle transition is this family failing to renegotiate?
- Am I genuinely multipartial, or have I quietly sided with someone?
- Is there violence, abuse, or untreated individual illness the systemic frame
  is hiding?
- If this symptom vanished tonight, what would the family have to face?
- Whose secret am I now holding, and does my no-secrets policy cover it?
- What generational pattern is repeating in front of me?

## Decision Frameworks

- **Who is the client, and what's the confidentiality contract?** Establish at the
  outset whether you treat the couple/family as the unit, and set a no-secrets
  policy (you won't keep individually disclosed secrets that affect the work) or a
  clear alternative — before anyone discloses an affair you can't then un-hear.
- **Screen for safety first.** Before any systemic intervention, screen for
  intimate partner violence and child abuse. Conjoint couples therapy is
  contraindicated where there's coercive control or fear; safety reframes
  everything, and you never treat violence as a mutual communication problem.
- **Modality by problem.** Structural work to realign a chaotic family's
  boundaries and hierarchy; strategic/solution-focused for a discrete stuck loop;
  EFT (emotionally focused therapy) for couples cycling in attachment distress;
  narrative to externalize a problem the family has fused with; Bowenian work for
  multigenerational, differentiation-driven patterns.
- **Whole system vs. subsystem.** Decide who's in the room — the couple, the whole
  family, the parents alone, an individual session within family work — and what
  each configuration risks (an individual session can breach the alliance with
  others if mishandled).
- **Refer out the individual.** When a member needs individual treatment — major
  depression, an addiction, trauma work — coordinate or refer rather than trying to
  do individual therapy inside family sessions.

## Workflow

1. **Convene the system.** Decide who needs to be in the room and get them there;
   the absent member is often the most informative.
2. **Contract.** Establish who the client is, the confidentiality and no-secrets
   policy, and the goals — for the system, in the members' own words.
3. **Join.** Accommodate to the family's style and pace until they accept you as a
   safe temporary part of their system.
4. **Assess the structure.** Map boundaries, alliances, hierarchy, triangles,
   loops, and life-cycle stage; screen for violence and individual pathology.
5. **Reframe.** Move the family from "fix him" to seeing the pattern they're all
   part of — the pivotal shift from linear to circular thinking.
6. **Intervene.** Apply the matched modality — realign boundaries, interrupt loops,
   enact new patterns in the room, externalize the problem, detriangle.
7. **Work the resistance.** When the system pushes back against change, treat it as
   homeostasis defending itself and address it directly.
8. **Coordinate and refer.** Link members to individual care as needed without
   abandoning the systemic frame.
9. **Consolidate and end.** Help the system hold its new structure across the next
   life-cycle demand, then step out of the system you joined.

## Common Tradeoffs

- **System focus vs. individual need.** Treating the pattern can underserve a
  member in real individual distress; treating the individual can lose the system.
- **Multipartiality vs. taking a stand.** Holding everyone's reality is the
  default — but coercive control, abuse, or a child's safety demands you stop
  balancing and act.
- **No-secrets vs. honest individual disclosure.** A no-secrets policy keeps the
  therapist usable but can suppress what a member would only say privately; a
  secrets-allowed policy invites collusion.
- **Pushing change vs. respecting homeostasis.** Move too fast and the system
  ejects you or relocates the symptom; move too slow and the family stays stuck.
- **Whose goals win.** A couple may want opposite outcomes — one to repair, one to
  exit. The therapist serves the relationship's clarity, not either agenda.
- **Joining vs. challenging.** Too much accommodation and nothing changes; too
  much challenge before joining and they don't come back.
- **Cultural humility vs. naming harm.** Respecting a family's structure can blur
  into tolerating a pattern that's hurting a member; know where humility ends.

## Rules of Thumb

- Treat the relationship, not the person who got sent.
- The symptom is doing a job — find the job before you take the symptom away.
- If you feel pulled to take a side, you're being triangulated; step out of the
  triangle.
- Join before you challenge; accommodation earns leverage.
- Screen for violence before you ever do conjoint couples work.
- When one person improves and the family gets worse, that's homeostasis, not
  regression.
- Set the no-secrets policy before anyone has a secret to tell.
- "He does X because she does Y" is always a loop, never one person's fault.
- The absent family member tells you the most.
- Symptoms spike at life-cycle transitions; ask what the family is failing to
  renegotiate.

## Failure Modes

- **Treating the identified patient.** Accepting the family's framing that one
  person is the problem and working only on them, relocating the symptom rather
  than resolving it.
- **Getting triangulated.** Being recruited as judge, ally, or rescuer for one
  member and losing the multipartial stance that makes change possible.
- **Linear thinking.** Hunting for who started it and assigning fault instead of
  mapping the circular loop.
- **The confidentiality trap.** Holding an individually disclosed secret (an
  affair, an exit plan) that makes honest couples work impossible — because no
  policy was set up front.
- **Missing violence.** Running conjoint therapy where there's coercive control,
  treating abuse as a mutual communication problem and endangering the victim.
- **Forcing change against homeostasis.** Pushing faster than the system can
  reorganize and getting ejected, or triggering a worse symptom elsewhere.
- **Importing the therapist's norms.** Mislabeling a culturally normal closeness
  as "enmeshment" and pathologizing a healthy family.

## Anti-patterns

- **The referee.** Refereeing who's right in a couple's fight instead of changing
  the pattern they're both caught in.
- **Individual therapy with an audience.** Doing one-on-one therapy on one member
  while the others sit and watch.
- **Secret-keeping by default** — agreeing to hold whatever any member tells you
  privately, then being unable to work honestly.
- **Blaming the symptom-bearer** — colluding with the family's scapegoating.
- **Premature challenge** — confronting the system before joining it and losing the
  family.
- **Symptom whack-a-mole** — fixing the presenting complaint with no attention to
  the structure that produced it.
- **Neutrality as detachment** — confusing multipartiality with passivity, leaving
  destructive patterns unaddressed.

## Vocabulary

- **Identified patient (IP)** — the family member who carries the symptom and is
  presented as "the problem," understood as the system's distress made visible.
- **Circular causality** — the view that behaviors mutually cause one another in
  loops, not in a one-way chain.
- **Homeostasis** — a system's tendency to maintain its equilibrium and resist
  change.
- **Triangulation** — drawing a third party into a two-person tension to stabilize
  it.
- **Differentiation of self** — the capacity to stay connected while retaining a
  separate self (Bowen).
- **Enmeshment / disengagement** — boundaries too diffuse / too rigid (Minuchin).
- **Multipartiality** — being allied to every family member's experience at once,
  rather than neutral or partisan.
- **Joining** — the therapist's accommodation to a family to be accepted as part of
  the system.
- **Reframing** — recasting a behavior or symptom in a new, systemic meaning.
- **No-secrets policy** — the agreement that the therapist won't keep
  individually disclosed information that affects the conjoint work.

## Tools

- **Genogram** — a multigenerational map of family structure, relationships, and
  patterns; the field's signature assessment tool.
- **Structural mapping** — diagramming boundaries, subsystems, alliances, and
  hierarchy.
- **Enactment** — having the family act out an interaction in session so it can be
  observed and altered live.
- **Circular and reflexive questioning** — Milan-style questions that surface the
  system's logic and differences in perception.
- **Externalizing conversations** — narrative techniques separating person from
  problem.
- **IPV and abuse screening instruments** — administered before conjoint work.
- **A model toolkit** — structural, strategic, Bowenian, narrative, and EFT
  interventions matched to the case.

## Collaboration

An MFT works with multiple clients in one room and multiple providers outside it.
They coordinate with individual therapists and mental health counselors (when a
member needs individual treatment alongside the family work), with psychiatrists
(for medication and individual diagnosis the systemic frame doesn't replace), with
pediatricians and physicians (whose patient is often the identified child), with
schools and child protective services (when a child's symptom or safety is
involved), and with substance abuse counselors when addiction is reorganizing the
family. The recurring friction is the boundary between systemic and individual
care: an individual provider may form an alliance that conflicts with the family
work, and information must cross the gap without breaching the multi-client
confidentiality contract. Within couples and families, the therapist is themselves
a temporary member of the system — the most delicate collaboration of all.

## Ethics

An MFT holds the confidences of several people who may have opposing interests and
wields influence over whether relationships continue. The duties (AAMFT Code of
Ethics): clarify who the client is and obtain informed consent from everyone in the
room, including the confidentiality and no-secrets policy, before treatment begins;
maintain multipartiality and avoid using the therapy to advance one member's agenda
against another's; never let the systemic frame excuse violence or abuse — screen
for it and prioritize safety over conjoint treatment; avoid the dual relationships
and boundary violations magnified when several family members are involved; respect
each member's autonomy and the family's cultural structure; and practice within
competence, referring for individual care the family frame can't provide. The gray
zones — whether to hold or disclose an affair under the chosen secrets policy, when
a child's interest overrides a parent's, how to act on a disclosed safety threat
without breaching others' trust, whether to support a couple's reconciliation when
one partner privately wants out — must be reasoned openly, consulted on, and
documented.

## Scenarios

**The "problem child" who isn't the problem.** Parents bring in a 14-year-old for
explosive anger, certain the boy is the issue. The novice starts individual anger
work with the teen. The expert convenes the whole family and watches: every time
the parents begin to argue with each other, the boy erupts and the parents unite to
manage him — the conflict between them vanishes. The symptom is doing a job. The
boy's anger is detouring the parents' marital conflict, stabilizing the system at
his expense. The therapist reframes — "I notice the two of you stop fighting the
moment he blows up; it's almost like his anger protects the marriage" — and shifts
the work to the parental subsystem and its conflict. Treating the boy alone would
have left the loop intact and the symptom looking for a new home.

**The affair and the no-secrets policy.** A couple comes for help with growing
distance. Because the therapist set a no-secrets policy at intake, when the wife
emails wanting an individual session to disclose an ongoing affair, the policy is
clear: the therapist can't hold it as a secret that would make the conjoint work a
performance. She tells the wife she can't keep it confidential from the marriage
work and helps her decide whether and how to bring it in. Had no policy been set,
the therapist would now be trapped — knowing something that makes every honest
couples session impossible while pretending not to. The decision wasn't made in the
crisis; it was made at the contract, which is exactly where who-is-the-client
questions have to be answered.

**Screening that overrides the systemic frame.** A couple presents with "constant
fighting" and wants help communicating. Before doing any conjoint work, the
therapist screens each partner separately and learns the wife is afraid of her
husband, tracks his moods to avoid setting him off, and has been isolated from her
family. This is coercive control, not a communication breakdown. The expert does
not run couples therapy — conjoint sessions can be dangerous, since the victim
can't speak freely and may be punished afterward, and treating abuse as "mutual"
legitimizes it. She pivots to safety: individual support and resources for the
wife, accountability-focused referral for the husband, and a safety plan. The
systemic, multipartial stance is the default — but violence is the line where it
stops, and recognizing that line is the difference between competence and harm.

## Related Occupations

A marriage and family therapist shares the clinical relationship of other
therapists but is defined by treating the relationship system rather than the
individual. Mental health counselors treat individuals with overlapping modalities
and refer family-system problems across. Psychologists add formal individual
assessment the systemic frame doesn't provide. Psychiatrists prescribe and diagnose
the individual conditions an MFT coordinates with. Substance abuse counselors treat
the addiction that often reorganizes a family the MFT is working with. Social
workers do overlapping family work with a stronger bridge to community and
basic-needs systems. Mediators help parties in conflict reach agreement but aim at
settlement, not at changing the relationship's underlying patterns.
