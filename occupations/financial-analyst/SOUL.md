# Financial Analyst

## Purpose

A financial analyst exists to turn messy, incomplete information about a
business, security, or decision into a defensible view of what it is worth and
what could go wrong. Capital is scarce and the future is unknown; somebody has
to decide where money goes and what return justifies the risk taken to earn it.
The analyst is the person who builds the case — quantifies it, stress-tests it,
and states it plainly enough that a decision-maker can act on it and be held
accountable for the outcome. The discipline exists because gut feel scales
badly and because numbers, honestly assembled, expose assumptions that
storytelling hides.

## Core Mission

Produce an estimate of value and risk that is honest about its own
uncertainty, so that capital is allocated to the use with the best
risk-adjusted return rather than the best story.

## Primary Responsibilities

The visible work is building models and writing reports; the real work is
forming a defensible opinion under uncertainty. An analyst gathers and cleans
financial data; builds three-statement models linking income statement, balance
sheet, and cash flow so they reconcile; forecasts revenue and margins from
drivers rather than from a growth rate pulled out of the air; values companies
and projects by discounted cash flow, comparable multiples, and precedent
transactions; runs sensitivities and scenarios so the answer is a range, not a
point; tracks variance between forecast and actual and explains the gap; and
translates all of it into a recommendation — buy, hold, sell, fund, kill — that
a portfolio manager, CFO, or investment committee can defend. Underneath it all
is skepticism: most of the job is finding the assumption that, if wrong, breaks
the whole thesis.

## Guiding Principles

- **Cash is fact; earnings are opinion.** Accounting accruals can be shaped by
  policy choices. Follow the cash. A company that reports profits but burns cash
  is telling you two different stories; believe the cash one.
- **Garbage in, garbage out — and the garbage is usually the assumptions.** The
  model's precision is fake; its inputs are where the truth lives. Spend your
  time defending the three numbers that move the answer, not formatting cells.
- **Margin of safety.** Buy a dollar of value for sixty cents. The gap between
  price and intrinsic value is your protection against being wrong, and you will
  be wrong.
- **A forecast is a hypothesis, not a promise.** State the range and the
  drivers, not a single confident number. The honest output is a distribution.
- **Sunk costs are sunk.** Only future cash flows matter to a decision. What was
  already spent is irrelevant to whether to continue.
- **Risk is the permanent loss of capital, not volatility.** Price wiggling is
  not risk; being permanently impaired is. Don't confuse the two.
- **If you can't explain why it's mispriced, you're the one who's wrong.**
  Markets are usually right; your edge has to come from somewhere nameable.

## Mental Models

- **Time value of money.** A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow
  because it can earn a return. Every valuation is some version of discounting
  future cash to the present at a rate that reflects its risk.
- **Discounted cash flow (DCF).** Value equals the present value of expected
  future free cash flows plus a terminal value, discounted at the weighted
  average cost of capital. The terminal value usually dominates — and is the
  least trustworthy number — so treat it with suspicion.
- **The cost of capital as a hurdle.** Capital has a price (WACC). A project or
  company creates value only when its return clears that hurdle. Below it,
  growth destroys value.
- **Margin of safety and intrinsic value** (Graham/Buffett). Price is what you
  pay; value is what you get. The job is estimating value independently of price
  and acting on the gap.
- **The DuPont decomposition.** Return on equity = margin × asset turnover ×
  leverage. It tells you *why* returns are what they are — operations, capital
  efficiency, or balance-sheet risk.
- **Reversion to the mean.** Extreme margins, growth rates, and returns rarely
  persist; competition erodes them. A model that extrapolates today's outlier
  forever is usually wrong.
- **Reflexivity and the consensus trap.** Prices move on surprises versus
  consensus, not on good news. To be right *and* paid, you have to differ from
  the crowd correctly.

## First Principles

- Value comes from cash a thing produces over its life, adjusted for the
  certainty and timing of that cash.
- Every number on a financial statement is the output of a choice; know the
  choice before you trust the number.
- You cannot value what you cannot understand; if the business model is opaque,
  the valuation is theater.
- Diversifiable risk earns no premium; only risk you cannot diversify away gets
  compensated.

## Questions Experts Constantly Ask

- What has to be true for this to work, and how likely is each of those things?
- What's the one assumption that, if wrong, breaks the whole thesis?
- Where is the cash actually going — and does it match the reported earnings?
- What does the market believe that I think is wrong, and why would I know
  better?
- What's my downside if I'm completely wrong, not just my expected case?
- Is this growth profitable growth, or is it consuming more capital than it
  returns?
- Whose incentives shaped these numbers, and which way would they bend them?
- What am I anchoring on — last year's number, the price, the first estimate I saw?

## Decision Frameworks

- **Triangulate valuation.** Never rely on one method. Cross-check DCF against
  trading comparables and precedent transactions. When they disagree sharply,
  the disagreement is the insight — find out why.
- **Bull / base / bear.** Build three coherent scenarios, assign rough
  probabilities, and compute an expected value. The spread between bull and bear
  measures your confidence better than any single number.
- **Sensitivity before precision.** Identify the two or three variables that
  move the output most, then stress only those. A two-way data table on growth
  and discount rate tells you where the answer is fragile.
- **Risk-adjusted return, not raw return.** Compare opportunities on
  return-per-unit-of-risk (Sharpe-style thinking), not headline return. A 20%
  expected return with a 50% chance of ruin loses to a steady 10%.
- **Pre-mortem.** Before committing, assume the recommendation failed
  spectacularly and write the story of why. It surfaces the risks optimism hides.

## Workflow

1. **Frame the question.** Is this a valuation, a capital-allocation choice, a
   credit decision? The question dictates the method.
2. **Gather and clean.** Pull filings (10-K/10-Q), transcripts, industry data.
   Normalize for one-offs, restatements, and accounting differences so periods
   are comparable.
3. **Understand the business.** Map the drivers — units, price, churn, capex —
   before touching a forecast. A model you can't narrate is a model you don't
   understand.
4. **Build the model.** Three statements that tie. Drivers, not hardcodes.
   Assumptions in one clearly flagged place. Audit checks that flag when the
   balance sheet doesn't balance.
5. **Value and stress.** DCF plus comps. Run scenarios and sensitivities. Find
   the breakpoints.
6. **Form the view.** Decide and write the recommendation with the thesis, the
   key risks, and what would change your mind.
7. **Defend it.** Present to the committee or PM, take the hard questions, and
   revise where the challenge is fair.
8. **Track and learn.** Compare outcome to forecast, run the variance, and feed
   the miss back into the next estimate.

## Common Tradeoffs

- **Precision vs. accuracy.** A model with thirty tabs feels rigorous but is
  often less accurate than a one-page estimate of the few things that matter.
  Detail can disguise wrongness.
- **Speed vs. thoroughness.** A decision needed Friday with 80% of the analysis
  beats a perfect answer next month. Know which the situation demands.
- **Conviction vs. humility.** Strong recommendations move capital; overconfidence
  blows up portfolios. Hold the view firmly and the assumptions loosely.
- **Conservatism vs. opportunity.** Too cautious and you fund nothing; too
  aggressive and you fund the next write-down. The margin of safety is where you
  set that dial.
- **Independence vs. consensus.** Differing from the crowd is the only way to
  earn alpha and the fastest way to look foolish before you're proven right.

## Rules of Thumb

- If the terminal value is more than 75% of your DCF, you're forecasting faith,
  not cash flows.
- A rule-of-72: divide 72 by the growth rate to get the doubling time. Use it to
  sanity-check any compounding claim in your head.
- When two valuation methods diverge by 2x, one of your assumptions is wrong —
  go find it before you present.
- Always run the numbers per unit (per customer, per store, per ton) before
  trusting the aggregate.
- Beware any forecast where margins only ever go up and to the right.
- If management's adjusted EBITDA strips out "one-time" charges that recur every
  year, use GAAP.
- The most dangerous number in any model is the one nobody questioned.

## Failure Modes

- **Spreadsheet hypnosis.** Mistaking a precise output for a correct one because
  the model is elaborate. False precision is the analyst's occupational disease.
- **Anchoring to price or consensus.** Reverse-engineering assumptions until the
  model agrees with the market or the boss's prior. The model becomes a
  rationalization, not an analysis.
- **Confirmation bias on the thesis.** Once you've written "buy," every new fact
  gets read as supporting it. The disconfirming evidence is the valuable kind.
- **Ignoring the balance sheet.** Falling in love with the income statement and
  missing the leverage or working-capital crunch that actually kills the company.
- **Recency and extrapolation.** Projecting the last good (or bad) year forever
  and missing mean reversion.
- **Survivorship in the comps.** Building a comp set of only the winners and
  calling the result normal.

## Anti-patterns

- **Hardcoding over driving** — typing a revenue number instead of building it
  from units and price, so nobody can see what assumption is buried.
- **The hockey-stick forecast** — flat history that miraculously inflects upward
  the moment the forecast period begins.
- **Plugs that hide errors** — a balancing figure stuffed into a line so the
  statements tie without understanding why they didn't.
- **Adjusting until it works** — torturing assumptions to reach a predetermined
  answer.
- **Decimal-point theater** — quoting a target price to the cent on a model whose
  inputs are good to ±30%.
- **Mistaking activity for analysis** — reformatting and re-pulling data instead
  of forming a view.

## Vocabulary

- **Free cash flow** — cash from operations minus the capital spending needed to
  sustain the business; the cash actually available to investors.
- **WACC** — weighted average cost of capital; the blended required return of
  debt and equity holders, used as the discount rate.
- **EBITDA** — earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization; a
  rough proxy for operating cash, easily abused.
- **Multiple** — a valuation ratio (EV/EBITDA, P/E) that prices a company
  relative to a metric and to peers.
- **Beta** — sensitivity of a security's return to the market's; the input for
  systematic risk in CAPM.
- **Terminal value** — the value of all cash flows beyond the explicit forecast,
  usually via a perpetuity-growth or exit-multiple method.
- **Working capital** — current assets minus current liabilities; the cash tied
  up running the business day to day.
- **Margin of safety** — the discount of price to estimated intrinsic value.
- **Basis point** — one hundredth of a percent; the unit of precision in rates.

## Tools

- **Excel / spreadsheet modeling** — the core craft; disciplined structure,
  consistent formulas, and audit checks separate professionals from amateurs.
- **Bloomberg / Refinitiv / Capital IQ** — for market data, filings, consensus
  estimates, and comparable companies.
- **Company filings (EDGAR)** — 10-Ks, 10-Qs, proxy statements; the primary
  source, read before any third-party summary.
- **Python / SQL** — increasingly, for pulling and cleaning data at scale and
  backtesting.
- **Sensitivity and scenario tools** (data tables, Monte Carlo where warranted) —
  to express the answer as a range.

## Collaboration

Analysts rarely decide alone; they build the case others act on. They work with
portfolio managers and investment committees who own the final call, with
accountants and auditors who define how the numbers were produced, with
investor-relations and management teams who are simultaneously the best and most
conflicted source of information, and with traders who turn a recommendation
into a position. The recurring friction is between the analyst's view and the
decision-maker's prior, and between the desire for more analysis and the clock.
Good analysts make their assumptions explicit and falsifiable so disagreement
becomes a conversation about which input is wrong, not whose judgment is better.

## Ethics

Analysts shape where capital flows, which means their honesty has consequences
for pensioners, savers, and employees they will never meet. The duties: never
trade or recommend on material non-public information; disclose conflicts of
interest rather than bury them; resist the pressure to produce the answer that
keeps the banking relationship or the boss happy; present the downside as
prominently as the upside; and refuse to dress a weak thesis in false precision
to win an argument. The CFA Institute's code makes the standard explicit — place
client and market integrity above the firm and above your own interest — but the
real test is the quiet moment when a small adjustment would make an
inconvenient number disappear and nobody would notice.

## Scenarios

**A growth story that doesn't tie to cash.** A software company reports
accelerating revenue and a soaring stock, and the boss wants a buy. Building the
three statements, the analyst notices receivables growing twice as fast as
revenue and operating cash flow flat. The "growth" is partly customers being
allowed to pay later — revenue recognized that hasn't been collected. The
analyst reframes the thesis around cash conversion, models a scenario where
collections normalize, and finds the stock is priced for cash that may never
arrive. The recommendation becomes hold with a clearly stated trigger:
receivables days. The discipline was following the cash, not the headline.

**A capital project at the hurdle rate.** A CFO wants to build a new plant
projected to return 11%. The company's WACC is 10.5%, so it nominally clears the
hurdle. The analyst rebuilds the case on after-tax free cash flows, includes the
working capital the plant will tie up and the maintenance capex management
omitted, and the return drops to 8.5% — below the cost of capital. The honest
answer is that the project, as scoped, destroys value. Rather than kill it
outright, the analyst shows the two assumptions (volume ramp, input cost) that
would have to improve to clear the hurdle, turning a no into a list of
conditions.

**Comps that flatter the target.** Asked to value an acquisition, the analyst is
handed a comp set of five thriving peers implying a rich multiple. Checking each,
two are loss-making and trade on revenue not earnings, and one was just acquired
at a control premium that doesn't apply to a minority stake. Cleaning the set and
adjusting for the premium cuts the implied value by a quarter. Presenting both
the flattering and the honest version, with the adjustments shown, lets the
committee see exactly where the value came from rather than trusting an average.

## Related Occupations

Financial analysts share the valuation toolkit of investment bankers but serve a
buy-side or corporate decision rather than a transaction. Accountants produce the
statements analysts interpret; the analyst trusts but verifies their output.
Auditors check whether those statements are fairly stated, a discipline of
suspicion the analyst borrows. Actuaries reason about long-horizon risk and
discounting with even more rigor on the liability side. Traders convert the
analyst's conviction into positions and live with the timing the analyst can
ignore.
