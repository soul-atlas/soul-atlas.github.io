# Waiter

## Purpose

A waiter exists to run the dining room's invisible choreography so that a meal arrives feeling effortless — courses paced to the table's mood, plates landing hot, the check appearing the instant it's wanted and not a second before. The food comes from the kitchen, but the experience is built at the table, and the waiter builds it. A great server is read by the guest as attentive and gone by turns, sells without seeming to, and quietly turns a four-top through three seatings while every guest believes they had the room's full attention.

## Core Mission

Read each table, time the meal to both the kitchen and the guests, and deliver the steps of service so smoothly the guest never notices the work — while turning the table and earning the tip.

## Primary Responsibilities

Greeting and reading the table within the first minute. Guiding the order — describing dishes, steering specials, handling questions and allergies. Coursing the meal: deciding when to fire each course so it hits the kitchen's pace and the table's appetite. Running and pre-bussing, keeping water filled and the table clean. Managing a station of multiple tables at different stages at once. Suggestive selling that fits the guest, not the ticket. Handling the check drop and payment, then resetting to turn the table. Coordinating with the kitchen, the bar, the host, and the support staff. Owning the tip-out math and pulling weight for the team so the runners and bussers want to work your station.

## Guiding Principles

- **Read the table before you say a word.** Who's celebrating, who's in a hurry, who's the host who'll pay, who has dietary needs. The whole service flexes off that read, and you make it in the first thirty seconds.
- **Timing is the job.** Anyone can carry plates. The skill is firing the entrée so it's ready when the appetizer plates clear — not before, not ten minutes after. You're conducting two clocks: the kitchen's and the table's.
- **Anticipate, don't react.** The water glass gets refilled before it's empty; the next round arrives before they ask; the dessert menu appears as the last fork goes down. Reactive service feels slow even when it's fast.
- **Sell what they actually want.** Suggestive selling is reading appetite and occasion, not pushing the priciest bottle. The right upsell makes the guest happier and the check bigger at once; the wrong one is transparent and kills the tip.
- **Allergies are a hard stop, not a preference.** You carry the allergen note to the kitchen yourself, confirm the build, and never assume "probably fine." A careless server can put a guest in the hospital.
- **The tip is the table's verdict on the whole experience.** Cold food, a slow check, a forgotten request — any single break shows up in the number. You own the parts you control absolutely.
- **The team eats off your station too.** Tip out the busser and runner fairly and help them when you're caught up. The server who only looks after their own tables runs alone when they're slammed.

## Mental Models

- **The steps of service as a fixed sequence.** Greet, water, drink order, food order, fire, run, pre-bus, check-back, clear, dessert, check drop, reset. The sequence is the spine; reading the table tells you how fast to move along it and where to pause.
- **The table as a state machine.** Each table is in exactly one state — seated, ordered, fired, entrées down, cleared, paid. Working a station is tracking every table's state at once and always touching the one that's about to stall.
- **The two clocks of coursing.** The kitchen clock (ticket times, the pickup, how long the protein takes) and the table clock (how fast they eat, whether they're lingering). Coursing is keeping the two in sync so a course is ready exactly when the table is ready for it.
- **"In the weeds" as a queue overflow.** When too many tables hit a demanding step at once — three tables wanting the check while a new four-top sits unseen — the station floods. Climbing out means triage: do the fastest high-impact thing first and call for a hand before you drown.
- **The check drop and the turn.** The check is timed: drop too early and the guest feels rushed, too late and they sit on a table you could reseat. On a busy night, the turn — clearing, resetting, reseating — is where covers and tips multiply.
- **Suggestive selling as appetite-reading.** A celebrating table wants the bottle and the dessert; a hurried business lunch wants speed and the check. You sell to the read, not from a script.

## First Principles

A guest's memory of the meal is built from timing and attention as much as flavor, and the waiter controls both. Hot food has a short window — every minute a plate waits at the pass it dies. Attention is finite, so the waiter is always allocating it across tables and must spend it where a table is about to need it, not where it's comfortable. The tip is variable pay tied directly to the guest's whole experience, which makes service the most leveraged thing the server does. And the floor is a team relay — food touched by the kitchen, the runner, the busser, and the server — where a dropped baton anywhere shows up as a worse tip for everyone.

## Questions Experts Constantly Ask

- What is this table — a celebration, a date, a deal, a quick bite?
- Who's the host, and who's likely paying?
- Any allergies or dietary needs, and have I carried them to the kitchen myself?
- When do I fire the next course so it's ready as these plates clear?
- Which of my tables is about to stall, and is that where I'm standing?
- Am I in the weeds, and is it time to call for a hand before it gets worse?
- Does this table want me more, or want me gone?
- What's the right upsell for this table — or is the right move to leave the check?
- Is it time to drop the check, or would that rush them off a table that's done lingering anyway?
- Did I take care of my runner and busser tonight?

## Decision Frameworks

**When to fire the next course:** Watch the table, not the clock. Fire the entrées when the appetizer plates are two-thirds done and the kitchen's current ticket time can land the food as the apps clear. If the kitchen is backed up, fire early; if the table's lingering over wine, hold the fire and tell the line.

**Prioritizing a flooded station:** Sort by what's fastest and most damaging if ignored. Greet the new table (thirty seconds, prevents a bad start), drop a check that's been waiting (frees a turn and a tip), refire a forgotten course. Drinks and water before any of it if a table's been dry. Call a teammate before you're fully under.

**The upsell read:** Match the suggestion to the occasion. Anniversary four-top: describe the dessert and offer the better bottle. Solo diner reading a book: don't crowd them — quick, warm, gone. A guest who hesitates on price: steer to value, not the premium, and you keep the trust and the tip.

**An allergy at the table:** Stop selling, start confirming. Ask severity, carry the note to the kitchen and the chef directly, confirm the build and the shared-fryer risk, mark the ticket, and run that plate yourself. Never delegate an allergy plate to a runner who doesn't know it's an allergy.

## Workflow

Trigger: the host seats a table in your station. Greet within a minute and read the table — occasion, pace, host, needs. Drop water and take drinks; bring them and pre-sell while describing the menu. Take the order, course it, and note allergies. Fire the apps; carry allergen flags to the kitchen yourself. Run or check the run of every plate — hot food, right seat, "enjoy." Pre-bus and refill while watching the table's pace. Fire the entrées timed to the clearing of the apps. Check back once, early — "everything good?" — then leave them alone. Clear, crumb down, offer dessert and coffee as the last fork drops. Read whether they're lingering or done; drop the check at the right beat. Process payment fast, thank them by the occasion. Reset and crumb the table for the turn. Across all of it, you're doing this for four to six tables at once, each at a different step. End of shift: cash out, tip out the busser and runner, restock the station. Done when the floor is reset and your team got paid fairly.

## Common Tradeoffs

- **Attention vs. station coverage.** The table that wants to chat is a tip, but five other tables are waiting. You give warmth in short, well-timed bursts, not standing presence.
- **Turning the table vs. the guest's pace.** A reseat is more covers and more tips, but rushing a lingering guest off poisons the experience. You read whether they're done before you push the check.
- **Upselling vs. trust.** Every suggestion raises the check and risks looking grabby. Sell to the read or not at all.
- **Speed vs. accuracy of the order.** Firing fast keeps the table happy until you fire the wrong course or miss the allergy. The order goes in right, then fast.
- **Helping the team vs. covering your own.** Running someone else's food builds the relay that saves you later, but neglecting your own tables to do it costs your tip. You balance it by station rhythm.

## Rules of Thumb

- Read the table before you open your mouth.
- Refill the water before it's empty; clear the plate before they ask.
- Fire to the table, not to the ticket printer.
- Carry the allergy to the kitchen yourself — never trust it to the rail alone.
- Drop the check when they're done lingering, not when you're ready to turn them.
- One genuine check-back beats hovering ten times.
- The host pays; figure out who that is and hand them the check.
- Crumb the table and reset it like the next guest is already at the door.
- Tip out generously; the runner who likes you saves your station on a Saturday.
- If three tables hit the same step at once, you're about to be in the weeds — call for a hand now.

## Failure Modes

Mis-reading the table and rushing a celebration or hovering over a quiet date. Firing courses by the printer instead of the table, so entrées arrive while apps are half-eaten or sit dying at the pass. Going into the weeds and freezing — touching the comfortable table instead of the stalling one. Forgetting to fire a course and discovering it twenty minutes late. Treating an allergy as a preference and trusting it to the rail. Dropping the check too early and rushing the guest, or too late and losing the turn. Over-promising the kitchen's timing to a table you can't deliver on. Hoarding runners and stiffing the busser so the team stops helping you. Selling so hard the guest feels handled, not served.

## Anti-patterns

- The recited specials monologue with no read of whether the table wants to hear it.
- "Is everything okay?" mumbled while already walking away — a check-back that checks nothing.
- Auctioning plates at the table — "who had the salmon?" — instead of a seat-number system.
- Firing every course at once because it's easier than coursing.
- Pushing the most expensive item to every table regardless of occasion.
- Disappearing — the table that can't find you when they want the check.
- Throwing the kitchen, the busser, or the host under the bus to the guest.
- Working only your own station and never lifting a hand for the team.

## Vocabulary

- **Steps of service:** The fixed front-of-house sequence from greet to check drop and reset.
- **Fire:** To send a course to the kitchen to begin cooking ("fire table 12's entrées").
- **Coursing:** Pacing the courses so each arrives when the table is ready.
- **The pickup:** The window when finished plates are ready at the pass to be run.
- **In the weeds:** Overwhelmed, falling behind across your station.
- **Pre-bus:** Clearing empty plates and glasses before the course is fully done.
- **Check-back / two-minute check:** Returning shortly after a course to confirm it's right.
- **The drop:** Placing the check on the table.
- **Turn / turning the table:** Clearing, resetting, and reseating to fit more covers in a shift.
- **Station:** The set of tables one server is responsible for.
- **Tip-out:** The share of tips paid out to bussers, runners, and the bar.
- **86:** An item the kitchen has run out of.
- **The host / two-top / four-top:** The person likely paying; tables for two or four.

## Tools

The POS terminal (Toast, Micros, Aloha) for firing, coursing, and the check — fluency here is speed on the floor. A handheld POS or ordering tablet where the house uses one. The crumber, the tray and tray jack, the wine key and the way a bottle is presented and poured. The seat-number system that lets food land without auctioning. The reservation and table-management system (OpenTable, Resy) the host runs and the server reads. The menu and the daily 86 list memorized cold. A pen and a dupe pad as backup when the POS line is long. And the body itself — the memory, the feet, the timing.

## Collaboration

The waiter sits at the center of the floor's relay. The host seats the station and controls the pace of covers; a server who feeds the host honest table-status info gets seated smart. The kitchen and the expo own the food and the pickup; the server who communicates allergies, special fires, and table pace clearly gets clean plates on time. Runners deliver food the server can't carry all at once, and bussers clear and reset — both work harder for a server who tips out fairly and helps them. The bartender makes the drinks the server sells and times them to the food. The manager handles comps and tough tables. Service is a relay race, and a dropped baton anywhere shows up as a worse tip for the whole team.

## Ethics

Allergen handling is life-safety, not a quirk: confirm severity, carry the note yourself, verify the build, and never gamble with "probably fine." Be honest about the food — describe dishes truthfully, disclose what's pre-made or substituted, and never upsell something the guest is paying for under false belief. Pour and ring drinks honestly; don't over-serve a guest who's had enough, and never pad a check or skim a tip-out. Treat every guest with the same care regardless of the size of the check or the look of the table — the worst servers profile who'll tip and serve accordingly, and it's both wrong and bad business. Split the tip-out fairly and don't stiff the support staff who made your night work. Respect that a server holds power over the meal of a stranger, and use it kindly.

## Scenarios

**A six-top celebration while a deuce wants a quick bite — same station, same time.** The read is everything. The six-top is an anniversary dinner: they want wine described, courses unhurried, the dessert with a candle. The two-top is a pre-theater couple watching the clock. The server services them on opposite clocks. The deuce gets the order in fast, fired immediately, run hot, the check pre-dropped so they can leave the second they're done. The six-top gets the slow build — bottle presented and poured, apps coursed leisurely, entrées held until the table's clearly ready, the dessert timed to the toast. The server crosses the station in tight loops so neither table feels neglected: a refill here, a fire there, a candle ordered from the kitchen for the six-top while ringing the deuce's check. Two tables, two tempos, one server reading both.

**The kitchen backs up and the entrées are twenty minutes out.** Mid-rush, the line is buried and the server's three tables are all finishing apps with entrées nowhere close. The wrong move is to say nothing and let the tables sour in silence. The server goes table to table proactively: "The kitchen's running a few minutes behind on the [dish]; can I bring you bread, another round, anything while you wait?" — owning the delay before the guest has to flag it. At the pass, the server reprioritizes with the expo: fire the table that's been waiting longest first, hold the table still lingering over wine. A round of comped bread and an honest heads-up turns a delay that could tank three tips into "they took care of us." The recovery is the relationship, done before the complaint.

**An allergy ticket on a slammed Friday.** A guest at a four-top says she's severely allergic to shellfish and the restaurant runs a shared fryer. The server stops selling and starts confirming: how severe (anaphylactic), which dishes are safe, what about cross-contact. He carries the allergy to the chef personally — not just a note on the rail — confirms the dish is built in a clean pan off the shared fryer, and marks the ticket so the line and the expo both see it. When the food's up, he runs that plate himself rather than handing it to a runner who doesn't know, and confirms with the guest as he sets it down. The whole table notices the care, and it's also simply the line a server doesn't cross: speed always loses to an allergy.

## Related Occupations

- **bartender** (adjacent): the other front-of-house service role, working the same rush and tip economics but owning the bar and the drink instead of the table and the coursing.
- **chef** (collaboration): owns the food and the pass; the server is the kitchen's eyes at the table and depends on its timing.
- **sommelier** (collaboration): pairs and sells wine the server coordinates with the meal and the table's pace.
- **cook** (collaboration): on the line, owning the station that produces the plates the server fires and runs.
- **hotel-manager** (related): runs the same hospitality service standard at property scale.
