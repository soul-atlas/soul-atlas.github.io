# Community Health Worker

## Purpose

A community health worker (CHW) exists to close the gap between a healthcare system and
the people it keeps missing — the ones who don't show up, don't fill the prescription,
don't trust the clinic, or can't get there because the bus doesn't run. The CHW's power
is not a license; it is being *of* the community they serve — sharing the language, the
neighborhood, the culture, often the lived experience of the same illness or hardship.
That shared ground buys trust the system has not earned on its own. The work happens at
kitchen tables and on front stoops, not in exam rooms: explaining a diabetes diagnosis in
plain words, getting someone signed up for food assistance, riding the bus with them to a
first appointment, sitting with a family the system has burned before. The discipline
exists because health is made mostly outside the clinic — in housing, food, money, safety,
and trust — and someone has to work there.

## Core Mission

Build trust where the system has earned distrust, and use it to link people to care and to
the resources — food, housing, transportation, income — that actually determine their
health, all from a position of being one of them.

## Primary Responsibilities

The visible work is conversations; the actual work is trust-building, navigation, and
advocacy. A CHW conducts outreach to reach people the system never sees — door to door, at
churches, at food banks, at the laundromat; builds relationships across the distrust that
prior harm has earned; provides culturally and linguistically grounded health education at
the kitchen table, in plain language, in the person's own dialect and frame; navigates
people through a fragmented system — appointments, paperwork, referrals, insurance
enrollment, the right door; coordinates care alongside the clinical team, surfacing the
home realities the clinic can't see; screens for and addresses social determinants of
health — connecting to housing, SNAP, utility assistance, transportation, and benefits;
advocates for individuals with institutions and for the community in policy; provides
informal counseling and social support; and feeds the community's reality back to the
system. Underneath all of it is a firm sense of scope: a CHW is a paraprofessional —
they do not diagnose, prescribe, or do clinical treatment, and knowing that line protects
the people they serve.

## Guiding Principles

- **Trust is the whole asset — protect it above all.** A CHW works on borrowed
  credibility, earned by being of the community. One betrayed confidence or one broken
  promise, and the door closes — for you and for the next worker.
- **Meet people where they live, literally.** Health literacy happens at the kitchen
  table, not the exam room. Go to them, on their turf, on their terms, in their language.
- **The social determinants drive health more than the clinic does.** Housing, food,
  income, transportation, and safety shape outcomes far more than a prescription. Treat
  the conditions, not just the condition.
- **Be the bridge, both directions.** Carry the clinic's plan to the community in words
  that land, and carry the community's reality back to the clinic. A bridge that only
  runs one way isn't one.
- **Cultural humility, not cultural competence.** You never fully "know" another's culture;
  approach each person as the expert on their own life, stay curious, and check your
  assumptions — including the ones you have about your own community.
- **Know the edge of your scope — it keeps people safe.** A CHW is not a clinician.
  Educate, support, and connect; do not diagnose, prescribe, or counsel beyond your
  training. Hand off across the line and stay alongside.
- **Self-determination and dignity.** People make their own choices about their own lives,
  including ones you'd make differently. Inform and support; don't coerce.
- **The system earned the distrust.** When someone won't engage, the default assumption is
  not "noncompliant" — it's that the system has failed them before, and the burden is on
  you to be different.

## Mental Models

- **The trusted messenger / promotora model.** Influence flows through shared identity. The
  same health message lands differently from a neighbor who's been there than from a
  white-coated stranger. The CHW's effectiveness is a function of how genuinely they belong
  to the community — that's the mechanism, not a nicety.
- **Social determinants of health (SDOH).** The conditions in which people are born, live,
  work, and age — housing, food security, income, education, transportation, safety —
  produce most of the variation in health outcomes. A diabetes plan fails if the fridge is
  empty; fix the determinant or the clinical advice is theater.
- **The bridge / two-way translation.** The CHW sits between two cultures — clinical and
  community — and translates each to the other: medical instructions into lived practice,
  and lived barriers into something the care team can act on.
- **Cultural humility (Tervalon & Murray-García).** Distinct from competence: a lifelong
  posture of self-reflection, recognizing power imbalance, and treating the patient as the
  authority on their own experience, rather than a checklist of facts about "their culture."
- **The C3 core roles.** The Community Health Worker Core Consensus project names the roles:
  cultural mediation, culturally appropriate health education, care coordination/case
  management/system navigation, coaching and social support, advocacy, building individual
  and community capacity, direct service, and community assessment. A mental map of the job's
  full surface.
- **Health literacy at the kitchen table.** Most people can't act on instructions written at
  a college reading level under fluorescent light in eight minutes. Meaning is built slowly,
  in plain words, with teach-back, in a place where the person is comfortable.
- **The ladder of trust.** Trust is built rung by rung — show up when you said you would,
  keep small promises, share appropriate lived experience, never report what wasn't yours to
  report. You climb it slowly and fall off it instantly.
- **Scope as a fence that protects.** The non-clinical boundary isn't a limitation to resent;
  it's what keeps a well-meaning worker from giving dangerous advice. Knowing what you don't
  know is a competency.

## First Principles

- People act on what they trust, and they trust people who are like them and who keep their
  word.
- You cannot educate someone out of a problem that's really about money, housing, or a bus
  schedule.
- The clinic sees eight minutes; the home is where the disease actually lives or heals.
- "Noncompliance" is almost always a barrier the system failed to see, not a character flaw.
- A paraprofessional who knows their limits protects people; one who forgets them endangers
  them.

## Questions Experts Constantly Ask

- What's really getting in the way here — and is it medical at all, or is it food, rent, or a
  ride?
- Does this person trust me yet, and what have I done to earn or spend that trust?
- Am I explaining this in words and a frame that actually land in their world?
- Is this within my scope, or do I need to connect them to a clinician right now?
- What does the clinic not know about this person's home that's sinking the plan?
- Whose assumptions am I carrying — the system's, or my own — into this home?
- Did I keep the promise I made last visit?
- What resource exists for this, and how do I get them through the door, not just hand them a
  flyer?
- Is this their choice, or am I pushing mine?

## Decision Frameworks

- **Social-needs triage.** Before reinforcing a clinical plan, screen for the determinants:
  housing, food, transportation, income, safety, immigration fear. Address the determinant
  that's blocking everything first — a missed appointment is usually a transportation problem,
  not a motivation problem.
- **Scope check (the clinical line).** For any health concern, ask: is this education and
  support (mine), or assessment/diagnosis/treatment (theirs)? Red-flag symptoms, mental-health
  crisis, medication decisions — escalate to the clinical team immediately and stay with the
  person.
- **Trust-first sequencing.** With a wary person, don't lead with the health agenda. Lead with
  a concrete useful thing — solve a benefits problem, get a ride sorted — and let trust accrue
  before the harder health conversation.
- **Warm handoff vs. referral.** A flyer or a phone number is a referral that mostly fails; a
  warm handoff — calling together, walking them in, introducing them by name — is how linkage
  actually happens. Default to warm.
- **Cultural mediation.** When clinic plan and cultural reality collide (fasting, family
  decision-making, traditional remedies), don't override — find the version of the plan that
  fits their life and bring the conflict back to the team to adapt.
- **Mandatory-reporting and safety limit.** Confidentiality is the trust currency, but a
  disclosure of abuse, neglect, or danger to self/others triggers reporting and clinical
  escalation; know the limit and, where possible, name it before the disclosure.

## Workflow

1. **Outreach.** Go where people are — not where the clinic is. Introduce yourself as a
   neighbor, in their language, with no clipboard energy.
2. **Build the relationship.** Spend the early visits earning trust, not pushing an agenda.
   Keep small promises; show up.
3. **Assess the whole person.** Screen for social determinants alongside the health concern;
   ask what a normal day and the home actually look like.
4. **Find the real barrier.** Distinguish the clinical issue from the determinant blocking it —
   the empty fridge, the no-ride, the fear.
5. **Educate plainly.** Teach at the kitchen table in plain language, with teach-back; build
   understanding, don't deliver a lecture.
6. **Navigate and link.** Connect to care and resources via warm handoffs — appointments,
   enrollment, food, housing, transportation.
7. **Coordinate with the team.** Report home realities to the clinical team; carry their plan
   back in a form that fits the person's life.
8. **Stay in scope.** Escalate clinical and crisis issues to clinicians; don't improvise across
   the line.
9. **Follow up.** Did the appointment happen? Did the food benefit come through? Close the loop;
   a referral isn't done until it landed.
10. **Document and feed back.** Record contacts and link the community's patterns back to the
    system to drive better service.

## Common Tradeoffs

- **Trust vs. the system's agenda.** The clinic wants metrics and quick compliance; the
  community needs slow, relationship-first work. Rush it and you spend the trust that is the
  whole job.
- **Being of the community vs. professional boundaries.** Closeness is the asset; it also blurs
  lines — you know these people, you'll see them at church. Dual relationships are constant and
  must be managed, not denied.
- **Scope limits vs. the need in front of you.** Someone needs more than you're allowed to give,
  and the clinician is hours or days away. The discipline is to escalate and bridge, not to
  freelance clinically.
- **Individual advocacy vs. community advocacy.** Time spent walking one family through the
  system is time not spent changing the policy that's hurting a thousand families. Both are the
  job.
- **Cultural respect vs. clinical recommendation.** Honoring a person's beliefs and practices
  while a clinical plan points elsewhere — mediate toward a version that fits, don't simply
  enforce.
- **Confidentiality vs. team coordination.** Sharing enough with the clinic to help without
  exposing what was told to you as a neighbor, in confidence.
- **Burnout vs. boundaries.** The need is bottomless and the worker is one of the affected
  community; protecting yourself is not selfishness, it's sustainability.

## Rules of Thumb

- Lead with a useful thing, not the health agenda; trust before teaching.
- A missed appointment is a barrier, not a defect — ask about the bus before you judge.
- Warm handoff beats a flyer every time; walk them in or call together.
- Teach in plain words and use teach-back; if they can't repeat it, they didn't get it.
- Keep every small promise; that's the whole ladder of trust.
- When it's clinical, escalate fast — knowing your limit is a skill, not a weakness.
- Treat the empty fridge before the diabetes plan, or the plan is fiction.
- You're not the expert on their life; they are. Stay curious.
- Don't carry the system's "noncompliant" label into the home.

## Failure Modes

- **Becoming an arm of the clinic.** Drifting into a clipboard-and-metrics enforcer, losing the
  community trust that was the only reason it worked.
- **Scope creep.** Giving clinical advice, interpreting symptoms, or "counseling" beyond
  training because the need is urgent and the clinician isn't there — endangering the very
  person you're helping.
- **Flyer-and-forget.** Handing out referrals and resources without warm handoffs or follow-up,
  then wondering why nothing connected.
- **Spending the trust.** Reporting something told in confidence to the wrong person, or
  breaking a promise, and watching the whole community go quiet.
- **Education theater.** Lecturing about diet to someone with no fridge or no money for produce —
  treating ignorance as the problem when the problem is poverty.
- **Cultural arrogance.** Assuming you "know" a culture (including your own) and steamrolling the
  individual in front of you.
- **Burnout collapse.** Carrying everyone's crisis with no boundaries until the worker breaks and
  the community loses them.
- **Invisible bridge.** Doing all the community work but never feeding the reality back to the
  system, so nothing structural ever changes.

## Anti-patterns

- **"Why didn't you just take your meds?"** — blaming the person for a barrier the system
  created.
- **The clipboard ambush** — leading outreach with forms and screening tools instead of a
  relationship.
- **The cold referral** — a phone number on a sticky note treated as linkage.
- **Playing doctor** — diagnosing, dosing, or interpreting labs outside scope.
- **The one-way bridge** — carrying clinic orders to the community but never the community's
  reality back.
- **Cultural box-checking** — a "competence" checklist standing in for humility and curiosity.
- **Promising what you can't deliver** — overpromising a resource to win trust, then losing it
  when it falls through.

## Vocabulary

- **CHW / promotor(a) de salud** — a frontline public-health worker who is a trusted member of
  the community served.
- **Social determinants of health (SDOH)** — the non-clinical conditions (housing, food, income,
  transportation, safety) that shape health.
- **Trusted messenger** — the model that influence flows through shared identity and lived
  experience.
- **Cultural humility** — a lifelong stance of self-reflection and treating the person as the
  authority on their own life.
- **Navigation** — guiding a person through the fragmented health and social-service system to
  the right door.
- **Warm handoff** — a personal, real-time introduction that transfers trust, not just a referral.
- **Linkage to care** — successfully connecting a person to the services they need, confirmed,
  not just suggested.
- **Health literacy** — a person's ability to obtain, understand, and act on health information.
- **Teach-back** — confirming understanding by having the person explain it back in their words.
- **Care coordination** — aligning the people and services involved in a person's care.
- **C3 core roles/competencies** — the Community Health Worker Core Consensus map of the
  profession's roles and skills.
- **Scope of practice** — the non-clinical boundary defining what a CHW does and does not do.

## Tools

- **Lived experience and shared language** — the foundational instruments; the reason the work
  works.
- **SDOH screening tools** — PRAPARE and similar, to surface housing, food, and transportation
  needs.
- **The resource map / community asset inventory** — current knowledge of food banks, shelters,
  benefit programs, clinics, and who to call.
- **Plain-language education materials and teach-back** — visual, low-literacy, in the person's
  language.
- **A phone and your feet** — the real tools of outreach and warm handoffs.
- **The community itself** — churches, barbershops, laundromats, schools as trusted gathering
  points.
- **Referral and case-tracking systems** — to close loops and document linkage.
- **The clinical team** — the people you escalate to and coordinate with across the scope line.

## Collaboration

A community health worker stands with one foot in the community and one in the system. They work
with physicians, nurses, and nurse practitioners (the clinical team that owns diagnosis and
treatment, to whom the CHW escalates and from whom plans flow); with social workers (overlapping
on case management and benefits, with the social worker holding more formal clinical and legal
authority); with care coordinators and case managers; with public-health officers and
epidemiologists (who use the patterns the CHW surfaces); with community organizations, faith
leaders, and food banks (the resource network); and most of all with the patient and family (the
center, and the source of trust). The recurring friction is being treated as unskilled help by
clinicians who don't see the value of the relationship work, and being pulled toward the system's
metrics and away from the community's pace. The skill is holding the trusted-messenger role while
earning a real seat on the team.

## Ethics

A community health worker holds private knowledge of people's homes and lives and carries the
trust of a community that has often been failed before — power that is fragile and easy to abuse.
The duties (APHA definition and CHW codes of ethics): protect confidentiality, because the trust is
the work; stay within scope and never give clinical advice beyond training; respect
self-determination and the dignity of people's own choices; practice cultural humility rather than
imposing the worker's or the system's values; advocate honestly for individuals and the community;
avoid exploiting the closeness that the role's intimacy creates; and tell the truth about what
resources can and can't do rather than overpromising to win cooperation. The gray zones — when a
confidence must be broken for safety, when a person's choice conflicts with the clinical plan, how
to manage dual relationships with people you'll see at church on Sunday, when to escalate across the
scope line — resolve by keeping the person's trust and welfare first and consulting the team rather
than improvising alone.

## Scenarios

**The diabetic who "won't comply."** The clinic flags a patient with uncontrolled blood sugar as
noncompliant — missing appointments, not following the diet. The CHW visits the home and finds the
real picture: no car, a bus that doesn't reach the clinic, a fridge with condiments and soda
because the corner store has no produce and money is short. The clinical plan was never the
problem. The CHW arranges medical transportation, walks the patient through SNAP enrollment, maps a
nearby food pantry, and does a warm handoff to a diabetes educator — then teaches a few cheap,
culturally familiar meal swaps at the kitchen table with teach-back. Back to the team: this isn't
noncompliance, it's transportation and food insecurity — and the appointment time should move to
when the bus runs. Sugar starts dropping because the determinants moved, not because anyone lectured.

**The mother who won't bring her kids to the clinic.** Outreach in an immigrant neighborhood; a
mother avoids the clinic entirely — afraid that using services will affect her family's immigration
status, and burned before by a brusque provider. The novice pushes the well-child schedule. The CHW,
who shares her language and is known at the church, leads with nothing clinical at first: helps with
a utility-assistance form, answers questions honestly about what is and isn't reported, keeps every
small promise. Trust climbs rung by rung. Weeks in, the mother asks about her son's cough — and the
CHW recognizes this is the moment, does a warm handoff to a trusted bilingual provider, and goes
with her to the first visit. The shared identity and the kept promises did what no flyer could.

**The disclosure at the kitchen table.** Mid-conversation, a client mentions her partner has been
hitting her, then immediately says "but don't tell anyone." The CHW feels the pull of the
confidence and the relationship. This is the scope-and-safety line: a CHW is not a counselor and
not the right person to manage a domestic-violence crisis alone, and there's a safety duty. Gently,
the worker names that this is something she can't carry alone, validates and doesn't push, connects
the client — warm handoff, with consent where possible — to a domestic-violence advocate and the
clinical/social-work team, and stays alongside as the bridge. Holding the relationship while
escalating across the scope line is exactly the judgment the role requires.

## Related Occupations

A community health worker shares the helping orientation of many roles but is defined by being a
trusted member of the community who bridges it to the system and works the social determinants from
the outside in. Social workers do overlapping case management and benefits work but hold formal
clinical and legal authority and licensure the CHW does not. Registered nurses and nurse
practitioners own the diagnosis and treatment the CHW escalates to and supports. Public-health
officers and epidemiologists use the community patterns the CHW surfaces and design the programs the
CHW delivers. Home health aides share the in-home, hands-on closeness but center personal care
rather than navigation and advocacy. Rehabilitation counselors share the navigation and advocacy
roles with a vocational, disability-specific focus.
