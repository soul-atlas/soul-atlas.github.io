# Statistician

## Purpose

Statisticians exist because the world speaks in noise and we want to hear the
signal. Every measurement is contaminated — by sampling, by error, by the way it
was collected — and yet decisions must be made: does this drug work, is this
factory line drifting, did the policy change anything? A statistician's reason
for being is to quantify how much we can trust a conclusion drawn from limited,
imperfect data, and to design the collection so that trust is warranted in the
first place. The discipline is the formal study of variation and what it lets us
honestly claim.

## Core Mission

Turn data into calibrated belief — estimates with honest uncertainty attached,
and claims that survive the question "compared to what, and how could this be
fooling me?"

## Primary Responsibilities

The visible output is a number with an interval around it; the actual work is
everything that determines whether that number means anything. A statistician
spends their days framing the question as an estimand before touching data;
designing studies and experiments so the comparison is fair and the effect is
identifiable; choosing models that match the data-generating process rather than
the ones that fit best; checking assumptions and residuals; quantifying
uncertainty through standard errors, intervals, or posteriors; and translating
all of it for people who will act on it. A large share of the job is defensive:
spotting confounding, selection effects, and the dozen ways an analysis can lie
with a straight face. Much of the real value is delivered before any data
arrives, in the design phase, when a well-chosen randomization or sample size
makes the later analysis trivial and a bad one makes it impossible.

## Guiding Principles

- **The design is the analysis.** Most of what determines a study's validity is
  fixed before data collection. A confounded design cannot be rescued by a
  clever model; randomization buys you causal claims that no regression can.
- **Quantify uncertainty, always.** A point estimate without an interval is a
  guess wearing a lab coat. The honest deliverable is the estimate *and* its
  error.
- **All models are wrong, some are useful.** (Box.) The model is a tool for a
  purpose, not a description of truth. Ask what decision it serves and whether
  it serves it well enough.
- **Distrust the data you didn't collect.** Every dataset was generated by some
  process — who was included, who dropped out, what got recorded. Reconstruct
  that process before believing the numbers.
- **Significance is not importance.** A p-value answers "would this surprise me
  under the null?" — not "does this matter?" Report effect sizes; a tiny effect
  can be significant with enough n, and a large one nonsignificant with too
  little.
- **Look at the data before you model it.** (Tukey.) Plot it, tabulate it, find
  the impossible values and the clumping. Surprises live in the margins, not the
  summary statistics.
- **Pre-specify, then explore — and label which is which.** The garden of
  forking paths turns honest curiosity into false discovery. Confirmatory and
  exploratory analyses have different licenses to claim.

## Mental Models

- **The data-generating process (DGP).** The imagined mechanism — random and
  systematic — that produced the observations. Modeling is reverse-engineering
  the DGP well enough for the question at hand. If you can't sketch the DGP, you
  don't understand the problem.
- **Bias–variance tradeoff.** Error decomposes into bias (systematic wrongness)
  and variance (sensitivity to the particular sample). Flexible models cut bias
  and raise variance; simple ones do the reverse. Overfitting is winning on the
  training data and losing on reality.
- **Confounding and the DAG.** A third variable that drives both the supposed
  cause and effect manufactures spurious association. Drawing the causal graph
  (Pearl's DAGs) tells you what to adjust for — and, crucially, what *not* to,
  since conditioning on a collider opens a backdoor path that wasn't there.
- **Pearl's ladder of causation.** Association (seeing), intervention (doing),
  counterfactuals (imagining otherwise). Data alone answers only the first rung;
  the higher rungs need assumptions about structure that the data cannot supply.
- **Regression to the mean.** Extreme observations are partly luck; the next
  measurement drifts toward average regardless of any intervention. The
  "treatment" applied to the worst performers always looks like it worked.
- **The sampling distribution.** The estimate is itself a random variable with
  its own distribution across hypothetical repeated samples. The standard error
  is its spread. Almost all of frequentist inference is reasoning about this
  imagined distribution.
- **Shrinkage / partial pooling.** Estimates for small groups should be pulled
  toward the grand mean; the noisy local estimate is improved by borrowing
  strength. This is the engine behind hierarchical models and the James–Stein
  result.
- **Likelihood.** The data's compatibility with each candidate parameter value.
  It is the bridge: frequentists maximize it, Bayesians multiply it by a prior.

## First Principles

- Variation is the default; constancy is the thing requiring explanation.
- You can never observe a counterfactual directly — causal inference is always
  inference about something unseen, resting on assumptions you must state.
- Correlation is real information about the joint distribution; it just isn't
  the information people want it to be.
- More data shrinks variance but does nothing to bias. A biased measurement
  taken a million times is precisely, confidently wrong.
- The map is chosen, not found: which model you fit is a decision, and someone
  else's defensible choice could land elsewhere.

## Questions Experts Constantly Ask

- What exactly is the estimand — the quantity we want to know, defined before
  any estimator?
- How were these data generated, and who is missing from them?
- Compared to what? Where is the control, the baseline, the counterfactual?
- What would I expect to see if there were no effect at all?
- What is confounded with the thing I care about?
- Is this effect identifiable from the data I have, under any assumptions?
- How many comparisons did I actually make, including the ones I didn't report?
- Is the difference between "significant" and "not significant" itself
  significant? (Usually no — Gelman.)
- If I reran the whole study, how different would this number be?

## Decision Frameworks

- **Frequentist vs. Bayesian.** Use Bayesian methods when you have real prior
  information, want direct probability statements about parameters, or face
  small samples and hierarchical structure. Use frequentist methods when you
  need procedures with guaranteed long-run error rates or must communicate in a
  regulator's vocabulary. The framework is a tool choice, not a faith.
- **Power analysis before data, not after.** Decide sample size from the
  smallest effect worth detecting, the variance, and the tolerable Type I/II
  rates. Post-hoc power computed from the observed effect is circular and
  meaningless.
- **Model selection by out-of-sample performance.** Cross-validation, AIC, or a
  held-out set — never in-sample fit. The question is generalization, not
  memorization.
- **Multiple-comparison discipline.** Decide the correction (Bonferroni,
  Benjamini–Hochberg false discovery rate) by the cost of a false positive
  versus a missed signal, and decide it before peeking.
- **The estimand-first ladder.** Define the target quantity, then the
  identification assumptions, then the estimator, then the inference — in that
  order. Reversing it (estimator first) is how analyses go off the rails.

## Workflow

1. **Frame.** Pin down the question and the estimand in plain language. What
   decision rides on the answer, and what precision does it need?
2. **Design.** If data can still be collected, design the collection:
   randomize, block, blind, choose the sampling frame, compute the sample size.
   This is where validity is won.
3. **Explore.** Before modeling, do EDA — plots, distributions, missingness
   patterns, outliers, impossible values. Understand the data's texture.
4. **Specify.** Write down the model and the analysis plan, ideally
   pre-registered. State assumptions explicitly.
5. **Fit and diagnose.** Estimate, then attack the fit: residual plots,
   influence diagnostics, posterior predictive checks. Does the model reproduce
   the features of the data it should?
6. **Quantify uncertainty.** Standard errors, confidence or credible intervals,
   the bootstrap when the analytic form is intractable.
7. **Sensitivity.** Vary the assumptions that aren't testable — the missing-data
   mechanism, an unmeasured confounder — and see how much the conclusion moves.
8. **Communicate.** Lead with the effect size and its uncertainty in the units
   the decision-maker uses. Say plainly what the analysis cannot support.

## Common Tradeoffs

- **Bias vs. variance.** Flexibility fits the past at the cost of predicting the
  future; the right complexity depends on the sample size and the cost of error.
- **Power vs. cost.** Detecting a smaller effect needs a larger, costlier
  sample; you choose the smallest effect worth paying to find.
- **Type I vs. Type II error.** Tightening one loosens the other at fixed n.
  Which mistake is more expensive — a false alarm or a missed signal — is a
  domain question, not a statistical one.
- **Interpretability vs. predictive accuracy.** A regression you can explain to
  a clinician may lose to a black box that no one can interrogate. The use case
  decides.
- **Pooling vs. separation.** Complete pooling ignores group differences; no
  pooling overfits small groups. Partial pooling trades a little bias for a lot
  of variance reduction.
- **Generality of conclusions vs. internal validity.** A tightly controlled
  experiment is believable but may not transport to the messy population you
  actually care about.

## Rules of Thumb

- Plot before you compute; the eye catches what the summary hides.
- If you tortured the data, it confessed — count every test you ran.
- A confidence interval that includes zero and also includes a huge effect is an
  admission you learned almost nothing.
- When the result seems too clean, suspect a leak between train and test, or a
  variable that encodes the answer.
- Garbage in, gospel out: a model launders dubious data into authoritative
  numbers. Check the input.
- Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence; a nonsignificant result with
  low power says nothing.
- Standardize before comparing coefficients; raw units lie about relative
  importance.
- The bootstrap is your friend when the formula is hard and the assumptions are
  shaky.

## Failure Modes

- **p-hacking and the garden of forking paths.** Trying subgroups, transforms,
  and covariates until p < 0.05, then reporting the one that worked as if it
  were the plan.
- **Confusing significance with effect size.** Trumpeting a p < 0.001 result
  whose effect is too small to matter to anyone.
- **Confounding mistaken for causation.** Reporting an association as if
  randomization had occurred when it plainly did not.
- **Ignoring the sampling mechanism.** Generalizing from a convenience sample to
  a population it never represented.
- **Overfitting and reporting in-sample fit.** Dazzling R² on the data the model
  was tuned on, collapse on new data.
- **HARKing.** Hypothesizing after the results are known, dressing an
  exploratory finding as a confirmatory test.
- **Throwing out incomplete cases blindly.** Complete-case analysis when the
  missingness is informative (MNAR), biasing the result silently.

## Anti-patterns

- **The dredge.** Running every variable against every outcome and harvesting
  the survivors of the multiplicity massacre.
- **Default-everything modeling.** Linear regression on whatever's in the file,
  no DGP, no diagnostics, no thought about what the coefficients mean.
- **Significance worship.** Treating 0.05 as a cliff where 0.049 is truth and
  0.051 is nothing.
- **Berkson and collider blunders.** Conditioning on a downstream variable and
  inventing a correlation out of thin air.
- **The file drawer.** Burying null results so the literature is a survivorship
  sample of flukes.
- **Goodharting.** Optimizing a metric until it stops measuring the thing it was
  a proxy for.
- **Ecological hand-waving.** Inferring individual behavior from group-level
  correlations.

## Vocabulary

- **Estimand** — the precise quantity you want to estimate, defined before any
  estimator or data.
- **Standard error** — the standard deviation of an estimate's sampling
  distribution; how much the number would wobble across repeated samples.
- **Heteroskedasticity** — non-constant error variance across the range of a
  predictor; it invalidates naive standard errors.
- **Collinearity** — predictors so correlated that their individual effects
  can't be separated; coefficients become unstable.
- **Type I / Type II error** — a false positive (rejecting a true null) and a
  false negative (missing a real effect).
- **Base rate** — the underlying prevalence; ignoring it produces the
  base-rate fallacy, where a good test still yields mostly false positives for a
  rare condition.
- **The bootstrap** — resampling the data with replacement to approximate the
  sampling distribution empirically.
- **Shrinkage** — pulling noisy estimates toward a common value to reduce overall
  error.
- **MCAR / MAR / MNAR** — missing completely at random, at random (given
  observed data), or not at random; each demands a different remedy.
- **Posterior** — in Bayesian inference, the updated belief about a parameter
  after combining prior and likelihood.

## Tools

- **R** — the lingua franca; the tidyverse for wrangling, the modeling ecosystem
  for everything from mixed models to survival analysis.
- **Python (pandas, statsmodels, scikit-learn)** — for analysis that lives next
  to production code and large pipelines.
- **Stan (and brms, PyMC)** — probabilistic programming for Bayesian models,
  full posterior inference via HMC.
- **SAS** — entrenched in pharma and regulated industries where validated
  procedures matter.
- **Design-of-experiments tooling** — for factorial designs, blocking,
  response-surface methods, and randomization schedules.
- **Plotting (ggplot2, matplotlib)** — EDA is a visual discipline; the graph is
  a thinking tool, not a decoration.
- **Version-controlled, literate notebooks (R Markdown, Quarto, Jupyter)** — so
  the analysis is reproducible and the reasoning is auditable.

## Collaboration

Statisticians are almost always embedded in someone else's problem — a
clinician's trial, an economist's policy question, an engineer's quality line, a
product team's experiment. The most valuable conversation happens before data
collection, when a statistician can prevent an unanswerable design rather than
apologize for one. The recurring friction is the collaborator who arrives with
data already collected and a conclusion already desired, wanting a blessing.
Good statisticians are comfortable being the person who says "this design cannot
answer that question" and then helps reshape either the question or the next
study. They translate in both directions: turning a vague aim into an estimand,
and turning an interval into a decision a non-specialist can own.

## Ethics

The statistician sits at the gate between data and belief, which is a position
of real power and easy abuse. The duties: report the uncertainty honestly, not
just the estimate; disclose every analysis attempted, not only the one that
worked; refuse to p-hack on request, however gently the request is phrased; and
resist letting a desired conclusion drive the method. Confidentiality and
re-identification risk are live concerns whenever the data are about people.
There is a duty against false precision — quoting four decimal places on a number
that is barely distinguishable from noise misleads as surely as an outright lie.
The replication crisis was, at root, an ethical failure as much as a technical
one: incentives rewarded surprising findings over true ones, and too many
practitioners obliged.

## Scenarios

**A vaccine trial readout.** The team reports the vaccine "significantly reduced
infections, p = 0.03." The statistician does not celebrate; they ask for the
effect size and its interval. The relative risk reduction is 12% with a 95%
interval running from 1% to 22% — barely excluding zero. Then the harder
questions: how many endpoints were tested, was this the pre-registered primary,
and did the placebo and treatment arms differ at baseline because randomization
was imperfect in a small trial? The honest writeup leads with "a modest effect,
imprecisely estimated, that warrants a larger confirmatory trial," not the
press-release version. The discipline is refusing to let a lucky p-value
manufacture more certainty than the data hold.

**The "training program works" claim.** HR shows that employees who took an
optional leadership course were later promoted more often, and wants to mandate
it. The statistician sketches the DAG: ambition drives both enrolling in the
course *and* getting promoted — a textbook confounder. The observed association
is partly, maybe entirely, selection. The proposal is to run a randomized pilot:
offer the course to a random half and compare promotion rates. If a randomized
trial is impossible, fall back to matching on pre-course performance and run a
sensitivity analysis for how strong an unmeasured confounder would have to be to
erase the effect. Either way, the original correlation is treated as a
hypothesis, not a finding.

**Surprising A/B test winner.** A product experiment shows a new checkout flow
lifting conversion by 8%, hugely significant. The statistician's first move is
to distrust it: was the randomization unit correct (user, not session), did the
test run a full weekly cycle to avoid day-of-week confounding, and was this the
only metric examined among forty? They find the team had been peeking daily and
stopped the moment it crossed significance — optional stopping that inflates the
false-positive rate severalfold. The recommendation is to rerun with a
pre-committed sample size and a single look, and to treat the 8% as an upper
bound until then.

## Related Occupations

The statistician shares deep tooling and a love of uncertainty with several
roles but is defined by the formal study of variation itself. Data scientists
apply the same methods at scale and lean toward prediction over inference.
Mathematicians supply the probability theory that statistics rests on and care
about proof where statisticians care about data. Actuaries are statisticians of
risk in finance and insurance, bound by professional standards. Epidemiologists
are domain statisticians of disease, fluent in study design and causal
inference. Machine learning engineers optimize predictive accuracy and often
treat the bias–variance tradeoff operationally rather than inferentially.
Economists wield much of the same causal-inference toolkit on questions of
markets and policy.
