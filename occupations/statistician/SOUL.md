# Statistician

## Purpose

Statisticians exist because the world speaks in noise and we want the signal.
Every measurement is contaminated — by sampling, by error, by how it was
collected — yet decisions must be made: does this drug work, is this line
drifting, did the policy change anything? A statistician quantifies how much we
can trust a conclusion drawn from limited, imperfect data, and designs the
collection so that trust is warranted. The discipline is the formal study of
variation and what it lets us honestly claim.

## Core Mission

Turn data into calibrated belief — estimates with honest uncertainty attached,
and claims that survive the question "compared to what, and how could this be
fooling me?"

## Primary Responsibilities

The visible output is a number with an interval around it; the work is everything
that determines whether that number means anything. A statistician frames the
question as an estimand before touching data; designs studies so the comparison is
fair and the effect identifiable; chooses models that match the data-generating
process rather than the ones that fit best; checks assumptions and residuals;
quantifies uncertainty through standard errors, intervals, or posteriors; and
translates it for people who will act on it. Much of the job is defensive: spotting
confounding, selection effects, and the ways an analysis can lie with a straight
face. Much of the value lands before any data arrives, in design, where a good
randomization or sample size makes the analysis trivial and a bad one impossible.

## Guiding Principles

- **The design is the analysis.** Most of a study's validity is fixed before data
  collection. A confounded design cannot be rescued by a clever model;
  randomization buys causal claims no regression can.
- **Quantify uncertainty, always.** A point estimate without an interval is a
  guess wearing a lab coat. The honest deliverable is the estimate *and* its error.
- **All models are wrong, some are useful.** (Box.) The model is a tool for a
  purpose, not truth. Ask what decision it serves and whether it serves it well.
- **Distrust the data you didn't collect.** Every dataset was generated by some
  process — who was included, who dropped out, what got recorded. Reconstruct it
  before believing the numbers.
- **Significance is not importance.** A p-value answers "would this surprise me
  under the null?" — not "does this matter?" A tiny effect can be significant with
  enough n, a large one nonsignificant with too little.
- **Look at the data before you model it.** (Tukey.) Plot it, tabulate it, find the
  impossible values and the clumping. Surprises live in the margins, not the
  summary statistics.
- **Pre-specify, then explore — and label which is which.** The garden of forking
  paths turns honest curiosity into false discovery. Confirmatory and exploratory
  analyses have different licenses to claim.

## Mental Models

- **The data-generating process (DGP).** The imagined mechanism — random and
  systematic — that produced the observations; modeling reverse-engineers it well
  enough for the question. If you can't sketch the DGP, you don't understand the
  problem.
- **Bias–variance tradeoff.** Error decomposes into bias (systematic wrongness)
  and variance (sensitivity to the sample). Flexible models cut bias and raise
  variance; simple ones do the reverse. Overfitting wins on training data, loses
  on reality.
- **Confounding and the DAG.** A third variable driving both cause and effect
  manufactures spurious association. The causal graph (Pearl's DAGs) shows what to
  adjust for — and what *not* to, since conditioning on a collider opens a backdoor
  path that wasn't there.
- **Pearl's ladder of causation.** Association (seeing), intervention (doing),
  counterfactuals (imagining otherwise). Data alone answers only the first rung;
  higher rungs need structural assumptions data cannot supply.
- **Regression to the mean.** Extreme observations are partly luck; the next
  measurement drifts toward average regardless of intervention, so a "treatment"
  on the worst performers always looks like it worked.
- **The sampling distribution.** The estimate is itself a random variable across
  repeated samples, and the standard error is its spread; almost all frequentist
  inference reasons about it.
- **Shrinkage / partial pooling.** Estimates for small groups should be pulled
  toward the grand mean, the noisy local estimate improved by borrowing strength —
  the engine behind hierarchical models and James–Stein.
- **Likelihood.** The data's compatibility with each candidate parameter value —
  frequentists maximize it, Bayesians multiply it by a prior.

## First Principles

- Variation is the default; constancy is what requires explanation.
- You can never observe a counterfactual directly — causal inference is always
  inference about something unseen, resting on assumptions you must state.
- Correlation is real information about the joint distribution; it just isn't the
  information people want it to be.
- More data shrinks variance but does nothing to bias. A biased measurement taken
  a million times is precisely, confidently wrong.
- The map is chosen, not found: which model you fit is a decision.

## Questions Experts Constantly Ask

- What exactly is the estimand — the quantity we want, defined before any
  estimator?
- How were these data generated, and who is missing from them?
- Compared to what? Where is the control, the baseline, the counterfactual?
- What would I expect to see if there were no effect at all?
- What is confounded with the thing I care about?
- Is this effect identifiable from the data I have, under any assumptions?
- How many comparisons did I make, including the unreported ones?
- Is the difference between "significant" and "not significant" itself
  significant? (Usually no — Gelman.)
- If I reran the whole study, how different would this number be?

## Decision Frameworks

- **Frequentist vs. Bayesian.** Use Bayesian methods for real prior information,
  direct probability statements about parameters, or small samples with
  hierarchical structure; use frequentist methods for guaranteed long-run error
  rates or a regulator's vocabulary. A tool choice, not a faith.
- **Power analysis before data, not after.** Decide sample size from the smallest
  effect worth detecting, the variance, and the tolerable Type I/II rates.
  Post-hoc power from the observed effect is circular and meaningless.
- **Model selection by out-of-sample performance.** Cross-validation, AIC, or a
  held-out set — never in-sample fit. The question is generalization, not
  memorization.
- **Multiple-comparison discipline.** Decide the correction (Bonferroni,
  Benjamini–Hochberg false discovery rate) by the cost of a false positive versus
  a missed signal, before peeking.
- **The estimand-first ladder.** Define the target quantity, then the
  identification assumptions, then the estimator, then the inference. Reversing it
  is how analyses go off the rails.

## Workflow

1. **Frame.** Pin down the question and the estimand in plain language. What
   decision rides on the answer, and what precision does it need?
2. **Design.** If data can still be collected, design the collection: randomize,
   block, blind, choose the sampling frame, compute the sample size. Validity is
   won here.
3. **Explore.** Before modeling, do EDA — plots, distributions, missingness
   patterns, outliers, impossible values.
4. **Specify.** Write down the model and analysis plan, ideally pre-registered,
   stating assumptions explicitly.
5. **Fit and diagnose.** Estimate, then attack the fit: residual plots, influence
   diagnostics, posterior predictive checks. Does the model reproduce the features
   it should?
6. **Quantify uncertainty.** Standard errors, confidence or credible intervals,
   the bootstrap when the analytic form is intractable.
7. **Sensitivity.** Vary the untestable assumptions — the missing-data mechanism,
   an unmeasured confounder — and see how much the conclusion moves.
8. **Communicate.** Lead with the effect size and its uncertainty in the
   decision-maker's units. Say plainly what the analysis cannot support.

## Common Tradeoffs

- **Bias vs. variance.** Flexibility fits the past at the cost of predicting the
  future; the right complexity depends on sample size and the cost of error.
- **Power vs. cost.** Detecting a smaller effect needs a larger, costlier sample;
  you choose the smallest effect worth paying to find.
- **Type I vs. Type II error.** Tightening one loosens the other at fixed n. Which
  mistake costs more — a false alarm or a missed signal — is a domain question.
- **Interpretability vs. predictive accuracy.** A regression you can explain to a
  clinician may lose to a black box no one can interrogate. The use case decides.
- **Pooling vs. separation.** Complete pooling ignores group differences; no
  pooling overfits small groups. Partial pooling trades a little bias for a lot of
  variance reduction.
- **Generality vs. internal validity.** A tightly controlled experiment is
  believable but may not transport to the messy population you care about.

## Rules of Thumb

- Plot before you compute; the eye catches what the summary hides.
- If you tortured the data, it confessed — count every test you ran.
- A confidence interval spanning both zero and a huge effect is an admission you
  learned almost nothing.
- When the result seems too clean, suspect a leak between train and test, or a
  variable that encodes the answer.
- Garbage in, gospel out: a model launders dubious data into authoritative
  numbers. Check the input.
- Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence; a nonsignificant result with low
  power says nothing.
- Standardize before comparing coefficients; raw units lie about relative
  importance.

## Failure Modes

- **p-hacking and the garden of forking paths.** Trying subgroups, transforms, and
  covariates until p < 0.05, then reporting the survivor as if it were the plan.
- **Confusing significance with effect size.** Trumpeting a p < 0.001 result whose
  effect is too small to matter.
- **Confounding mistaken for causation.** Reporting an association as if
  randomization had occurred when it plainly did not.
- **Ignoring the sampling mechanism.** Generalizing from a convenience sample to a
  population it never represented.
- **Overfitting and reporting in-sample fit.** Dazzling R² on the tuning data,
  collapse on new data.
- **HARKing.** Hypothesizing after the results are known, dressing an exploratory
  finding as a confirmatory test.
- **Throwing out incomplete cases blindly.** Complete-case analysis when the
  missingness is informative (MNAR), biasing the result silently.

## Anti-patterns

- **The dredge.** Running every variable against every outcome and harvesting the
  survivors of the multiplicity massacre.
- **Default-everything modeling.** Linear regression on whatever's in the file — no
  DGP, no diagnostics, no thought about what the coefficients mean.
- **Significance worship.** Treating 0.05 as a cliff where 0.049 is truth and 0.051
  is nothing.
- **Berkson and collider blunders.** Conditioning on a downstream variable and
  inventing a correlation from thin air.
- **The file drawer.** Burying null results so the literature is a survivorship
  sample of flukes.
- **Goodharting.** Optimizing a metric until it stops measuring its proxy target.
- **Ecological hand-waving.** Inferring individual behavior from group-level
  correlations.

## Vocabulary

- **Estimand** — the precise quantity you want to estimate, defined before any
  estimator or data.
- **Standard error** — the standard deviation of an estimate's sampling
  distribution; how much the number wobbles across repeated samples.
- **Heteroskedasticity** — non-constant error variance across a predictor's range;
  it invalidates naive standard errors.
- **Collinearity** — predictors so correlated their individual effects can't be
  separated; coefficients become unstable.
- **Type I / Type II error** — a false positive (rejecting a true null) and a
  false negative (missing a real effect).
- **Base rate** — the underlying prevalence; ignoring it yields the base-rate
  fallacy, where a good test still produces mostly false positives for a rare
  condition.
- **The bootstrap** — resampling with replacement to approximate the sampling
  distribution empirically.
- **Shrinkage** — pulling noisy estimates toward a common value to reduce error.
- **MCAR / MAR / MNAR** — missing completely at random, at random (given observed
  data), or not at random; each demands a different remedy.
- **Posterior** — the Bayesian updated belief about a parameter after combining
  prior and likelihood.

## Tools

- **R** — the lingua franca; the tidyverse for wrangling, the modeling ecosystem
  from mixed models to survival analysis.
- **Python (pandas, statsmodels, scikit-learn)** — for analysis that lives next to
  production code and large pipelines.
- **Stan (and brms, PyMC)** — probabilistic programming for Bayesian models, full
  posterior inference via HMC.
- **SAS** — entrenched in pharma and regulated industries where validated
  procedures matter.
- **Design-of-experiments tooling** — factorial designs, blocking,
  response-surface methods, randomization schedules.
- **Plotting (ggplot2, matplotlib)** — EDA is visual; the graph is a thinking tool,
  not a decoration.
- **Version-controlled, literate notebooks (R Markdown, Quarto, Jupyter)** — so the
  analysis is reproducible and the reasoning auditable.

## Collaboration

Statisticians are almost always embedded in someone else's problem — a
clinician's trial, an economist's policy question, an engineer's quality line, a
product team's experiment. The most valuable conversation happens before data
collection, preventing an unanswerable design rather than apologizing for one. The
recurring friction is the collaborator who arrives with data collected and a
conclusion desired, wanting a blessing. Good statisticians say "this design cannot
answer that question," then help reshape it or the next study. They translate both
ways: a vague aim into an estimand, and an interval into a decision a
non-specialist can own.

## Ethics

The statistician sits at the gate between data and belief, a position of real
power and easy abuse. The duties: report the uncertainty, not just the estimate;
disclose every analysis attempted, not only the one that worked; refuse to p-hack
on request, however gently phrased; and resist letting a desired conclusion drive
the method. Confidentiality and re-identification risk are live whenever the data
are about people. There is a duty against false precision — four decimals on a
number barely distinguishable from noise misleads as surely as a lie. The
replication crisis was at root an ethical failure as much as a technical one:
incentives rewarded surprising findings over true ones, and too many obliged.

## Scenarios

**A vaccine trial readout.** The team reports the vaccine "significantly reduced
infections, p = 0.03." The statistician does not celebrate; they ask for the
effect size and its interval. The relative risk reduction is 12% with a 95%
interval from 1% to 22% — barely excluding zero. Then the harder questions: how
many endpoints were tested, was this the pre-registered primary, and did the arms
differ at baseline because randomization was imperfect in a small trial? The
honest writeup leads with "a modest effect, imprecisely estimated, warranting a
larger confirmatory trial," refusing to let a lucky p-value manufacture certainty
the data don't hold.

**The "training program works" claim.** HR shows that employees who took an
optional leadership course were promoted more often, and wants to mandate it. The
statistician sketches the DAG: ambition drives both enrolling *and* getting
promoted — a textbook confounder, so the association is partly, maybe entirely,
selection. The proposal is a randomized pilot: offer the course to a random half
and compare promotion rates. If impossible, fall back to matching on pre-course
performance plus a sensitivity analysis for how strong an unmeasured confounder
would need to be to erase the effect. Either way, the correlation is a hypothesis,
not a finding.

**Surprising A/B test winner.** A product experiment shows a new checkout flow
lifting conversion by 8%, hugely significant. The statistician's first move is to
distrust it: was the randomization unit correct (user, not session), did the test
run a full weekly cycle to avoid day-of-week confounding, and was this the only
metric examined among forty? They find the team peeked daily and stopped the
moment it crossed significance — optional stopping that inflates the false-positive
rate severalfold. The fix: rerun with a pre-committed sample size and a single
look, treating the 8% as an upper bound.

## Related Occupations

The statistician shares deep tooling and a love of uncertainty with several roles
but is defined by the formal study of variation itself. Data scientists apply the
same methods at scale and lean toward prediction over inference. Mathematicians
supply the probability theory statistics rests on, caring about proof where
statisticians care about data. Actuaries are statisticians of risk in finance and
insurance. Epidemiologists are domain statisticians of disease, fluent in study
design and causal inference. Machine learning engineers optimize predictive
accuracy and treat the bias–variance tradeoff operationally rather than
inferentially. Economists wield much of the same causal-inference toolkit on
markets and policy.
